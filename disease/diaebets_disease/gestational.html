Gestational

  • Nalamaree Team
  • 24 April 2024

Overview

Gestational diabetes (GD) is a type of diabetes that develops during pregnancy in women who did not have diabetes before. It occurs when your body cannot produce enough insulin or use it effectively, leading to high blood sugar levels. This can harm both you and your baby if left untreated.

Causes

Gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM) is a condition characterized by high blood sugar levels that develop during pregnancy. It occurs when the body cannot produce enough insulin to meet the increased demands of pregnancy, leading to insulin resistance. Insulin is a hormone produced by the pancreas that helps regulate blood sugar levels by facilitating the uptake of glucose into cells for energy production.

Several factors contribute to the development of gestational diabetes:

  • Hormonal changes: During pregnancy, the placenta produces hormones that help sustain the pregnancy. Some of these hormones can interfere with insulin's action, causing insulin resistance. As a result, the mother's body needs more insulin to process glucose effectively.
  • Increased insulin resistance: Insulin resistance naturally occurs during pregnancy to ensure an adequate supply of glucose to the fetus. However, in some women, this resistance becomes more pronounced, leading to elevated blood sugar levels.
  • Genetic predisposition: Women with a family history of type 2 diabetes or a personal history of gestational diabetes are at a higher risk of developing GDM. Genetic factors can influence insulin sensitivity and predispose individuals to glucose intolerance.
  • Obesity or overweight: Excess weight before pregnancy or significant weight gain during pregnancy can increase the risk of gestational diabetes. Adipose tissue releases hormones and cytokines that promote insulin resistance, contributing to elevated blood sugar levels.
  • Age: Women over the age of 25, particularly those over 35, are at a higher risk of developing gestational diabetes. Aging is associated with decreased insulin sensitivity, making older women more susceptible to glucose intolerance during pregnancy.
  • Ethnicity: Certain ethnic groups, such as African Americans, Hispanics, Native Americans, and South Asians, have a higher prevalence of gestational diabetes compared to others. Genetic and lifestyle factors within these populations may contribute to increased insulin resistance and glucose intolerance.
  • Previous history of gestational diabetes: Women who have previously experienced gestational diabetes in a past pregnancy are at an increased risk of developing it again in subsequent pregnancies.

    • Symptoms

    These symptoms can include:

  • Increased thirst (polydipsia): Feeling unusually thirsty and needing to drink more fluids than usual.
  • Frequent urination (polyuria): Needing to urinate more frequently than usual, particularly during the night.
  • Fatigue: Feeling unusually tired or lacking in energy despite adequate rest.
  • Increased hunger (polyphagia): Feeling hungrier than usual or experiencing more frequent hunger pangs.
  • Blurred vision: Vision may become blurry due to fluctuations in blood sugar levels.
  • Yeast infections: Women with gestational diabetes may be more prone to yeast infections, such as vaginal candidiasis.

    • Treatment: Modern Medicine

    The primary treatment for gestational diabetes revolves around lifestyle modifications, including diet and exercise, to control blood sugar levels. However, in some cases where lifestyle changes are not sufficient to maintain glucose levels within the target range, medication may be prescribed. The two main types of medication used to treat gestational diabetes are insulin and oral medications.

  • Insulin: Insulin therapy is the most common treatment for gestational diabetes when diet and exercise alone are insufficient to control blood sugar levels. Insulin is safe for use during pregnancy and does not cross the placenta to affect the baby. Several types of insulin may be used, including rapid-acting, short-acting, intermediate-acting, and long-acting insulin. The type and dosage of insulin prescribed depend on individual blood sugar levels and insulin requirements.
  • Oral medications: While insulin is the preferred medication for treating gestational diabetes, some oral medications may be considered as alternatives in certain situations. The most commonly used oral medication for gestational diabetes is Metformin. Metformin works by decreasing glucose production in the liver and improving insulin sensitivity in the body's cells. However, the use of oral medications for gestational diabetes is less common than insulin and may not be suitable for all women.

    • Treatment: Traditional Medicine

  • Herbal remedies: Some herbs and supplements are purported to help regulate blood sugar levels. However, evidence supporting their effectiveness and safety during pregnancy is often limited. Examples include cinnamon, Fenugreek, bitter melon, and Gymnema Sylvestre. It's essential to use caution with herbal remedies, as they may have side effects or interact with medications.
  • Dietary supplements: Some dietary supplements, such as omega-3 fatty acids, vitamin D, and Probiotics, have been studied for their potential benefits in managing blood sugar levels and improving insulin sensitivity. However, more research is needed to determine their effectiveness and safety specifically for gestational diabetes.

    • Caution

    For mothers:

  • Higher risk of preeclampsia: This condition involves high blood pressure and can have serious complications for both mother and baby.
  • Increased risk of needing a cesarean section: If your baby is large or blood sugar control is challenging, a cesarean section might be necessary.
  • Increased risk of developing type 2 diabetes later in life: Having GD puts you at higher risk for developing type 2 diabetes later, even if your blood sugar levels normalize after pregnancy.
  • High blood pressure: This can increase the risk of other health problems like heart disease and stroke.

    • For babies:

  • Large birth weight: This can make delivery more difficult and increase the risk of injuries for both mother and baby.
  • Low blood sugar after birth: Babies born to mothers with GD may have low blood sugar after birth, requiring monitoring and treatment.
  • Increased risk of obesity and type 2 diabetes later in life: Babies born to mothers with GD are more likely to develop these conditions later in life.

    • Prevention

  • Stress reduction techniques: Stress can affect blood sugar levels, so techniques to reduce stress, such as mindfulness meditation, deep breathing exercises, yoga, and progressive muscle relaxation, may be beneficial for women with gestational diabetes. These techniques can help promote relaxation and improve overall well-being during pregnancy.
  • Adequate sleep: Getting enough sleep is essential for overall health and may help regulate blood sugar levels. Pregnant women should aim for seven to nine hours of sleep per night and establish a regular sleep schedule to support optimal blood sugar control.
  • Exercise regularly: Engage in moderate-intensity exercise most days of the week, as recommended by your healthcare provider. Activities such as brisk walking, swimming, and prenatal yoga can help improve insulin sensitivity and control weight gain during pregnancy.
  • Monitor blood sugar levels: If you have risk factors for gestational diabetes or a history of the condition in a previous pregnancy, talk to your healthcare provider about early screening. Monitoring blood sugar levels can help detect gestational diabetes early, allowing for prompt intervention and management.
  • Eat a balanced diet: Prioritize nutrient-rich foods, including fruits, vegetables, whole grains, lean proteins, and healthy fats. Avoid excessive consumption of sugary and processed foods, which can spike blood sugar levels. A registered dietitian can provide personalized guidance on meal planning.
