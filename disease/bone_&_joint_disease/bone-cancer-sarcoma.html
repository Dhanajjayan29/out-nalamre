Bone Cancer / Sarcoma

  • Nalamaree Team
  • 25 April 2024

Overview

Sarcoma is a type of cancer that develops in the bones and soft tissues of the body, such as muscles, fat, tendons, and blood vessels. It can occur in any part of the body and can affect people of all ages. Sarcomas are divided into two main groups: bone sarcomas (which develop in the bone) and soft tissue sarcomas (which develop in soft tissues).

There are several sub types of sarcoma, each with its own unique characteristics and behaviors. Some examples of sarcoma sub types include

  • Osteosarcoma: This type of sarcoma originates in the bone and is most commonly found in children and young adults. It often occurs in the long bones of the arms and legs.
  • Chondrosarcoma: Chondrosarcoma also arises in bone tissue, specifically in the cartilage. It most frequently affects adults and tends to occur in the pelvis, hip, and shoulder regions.
  • Leiomyosarcoma: This sarcoma develops in smooth muscle tissue and can arise in various parts of the body, including the uterus, gastrointestinal tract, and blood vessels. It's more common in adults.
  • Liposarcoma: Liposarcoma originates in fat cells and typically occurs in the limbs or abdomen. It's one of the most common types of soft tissue sarcoma in adults.

    • Causes

    Sarcoma is a type of cancer that originates in the cells of connective tissue, such as bones, muscles, cartilage, nerves, fat, and blood vessels. There are several ways in which sarcoma can develop and cause harm to a person:

  • Genetic Factors: Certain genetic mutations or inherited conditions can increase the risk of developing sarcoma. Examples include Li-Fraumeni syndrome, hereditary retinoblastoma, neuro fibromatosis type 1, and Gardner syndrome.
  • Environmental Factors: Exposure to certain environmental factors, such as radiation or certain chemicals, may increase the risk of developing sarcoma. For example, people exposed to high doses of ionizing radiation (such as radiation therapy for other cancers) have an increased risk of developing sarcoma.
  • Unknown Causes: In many cases, the exact cause of sarcoma is unknown. It may develop spontaneously without any known genetic or environmental factors contributing to its development.
  • Soft Tissue Sarcoma: Soft tissue sarcomas can arise from various types of soft tissues, including muscles, tendons, fat, blood vessels, and nerves. They may present as a painless lump or swelling, often growing gradually over time. As they grow, they can compress nearby structures and interfere with normal organ function.
  • Bone Sarcoma: Bone sarcomas, such as osteosarcoma and Ewing sarcoma, originate in the bones. They often cause bone pain, swelling, and sometimes fractures. As they grow, they can weaken the affected bone and spread to other parts of the body.
  • Metastasis: Sarcomas can spread (metastasize) to other parts of the body, such as the lungs, liver, or other bones, where they can form secondary tumors. This can lead to additional symptoms depending on the location of the metastases.

    • Symptoms

  • Lump or Mass: The most common symptom of sarcoma is the development of a lump or mass, which may be painless or tender to the touch. The lump may increase in size over time.
  • Swelling: Swelling may occur in the affected area, especially if the sarcoma is located in the limbs.
  • Pain: Sarcomas can cause localized pain, which may worsen over time, especially if the tumor grows and presses on surrounding tissues or nerves. However, not all sarcomas cause pain, especially in the early stages.
  • Limited Range of Motion: If the sarcoma is located near a joint or muscle, it may cause stiffness or limited range of motion in that area.
  • Bone Pain: Bone sarcomas, such as osteosarcoma, may cause bone pain, which can worsen at night or with activity.
  • Fractures: Bone sarcomas can weaken the affected bone, increasing the risk of fractures, even with minor trauma.
  • Neurological Symptoms: If a sarcoma compresses nerves or other structures, it can lead to neurological symptoms such as numbness, tingling, weakness, or loss of function in the affected area.
  • Abdominal Symptoms: Sarcomas in the abdomen or retro peritoneum (the area behind the abdomen) may cause abdominal pain, bloating, or other gastrointestinal symptoms if they press on nearby organs.
  • Breathing Difficulties: Sarcomas in the chest cavity may cause symptoms such as shortness of breath, chest pain, or coughing if they affect the lungs or surrounding structures.

    • Treatment: Modern Medicine

    Here are the primary treatment methods for sarcoma:

  • Surgery: Surgery is often the main treatment for sarcoma and aims to remove the tumor and a margin of healthy tissue surrounding it. The extent of surgery depends on factors such as the size and location of the tumor. In some cases, limb-sparing surgery may be possible to preserve function and appearance.
  • Radiation Therapy: Radiation therapy uses high-energy beams to target and destroy cancer cells. It may be used before surgery (neoadjuvant radiation therapy) to shrink the tumor and make it easier to remove, or after surgery (adjuvant radiation therapy) to kill any remaining cancer cells and reduce the risk of recurrence.
  • Chemotherapy: Chemotherapy uses drugs to kill cancer cells or stop them from growing. It may be used before surgery (neoadjuvant chemotherapy) to shrink the tumor, after surgery (adjuvant chemotherapy) to kill any remaining cancer cells, or as the primary treatment for advanced or metastatic sarcoma.
  • Targeted Therapy: Targeted therapy drugs specifically target certain molecules or pathways involved in cancer growth and progression. These drugs may be used in combination with other treatments or as a standalone therapy for certain types of sarcoma.
  • Immunotherapy: Immunotherapy drugs help the immune system recognize and attack cancer cells. While immunotherapy has shown promise in some types of cancer, its role in the treatment of sarcoma is still being investigated in clinical trials.

    • Treatment: Traditional Medicine

  • Acupuncture: Some people find acupuncture helpful in managing pain and reducing treatment-related side effects such as nausea.
  • Herbal remedies: Certain herbal supplements or teas may be used as complementary treatments, though it's critical to discuss these with a healthcare provider due to potential interactions with conventional treatments.
  • Massage therapy: Massage can be used to relieve muscle tension, reduce stress, and improve overall well-being.
  • Mind-body practices: Practices such as meditation, yoga, and tai chi can help reduce stress, improve sleep, and promote a sense of calm.

    • Caution

  • Early Detection: Sarcomas can sometimes be difficult to diagnose because they can develop in deep tissues and may initially cause vague symptoms or be mistaken for other conditions. However, it's important to seek medical attention if you notice any persistent symptoms such as a lump or mass, unexplained pain or swelling, or changes in the appearance or function of a limb.
  • Multidisciplinary Care: Sarcoma treatment often requires a multidisciplinary approach involving oncologists, surgeons, radiation oncologists, pathologists, and other specialists. It's important to seek care from a medical center with expertise in sarcoma diagnosis and treatment to ensure that you receive comprehensive and coordinated care.

    • Prevention

  • Minimizing exposure to radiation: Limiting exposure to unnecessary radiation, including medical imaging such as X-rays and CT scans, can help reduce potential risk factors for sarcoma.
  • Avoiding harmful environmental exposures: Being cautious about exposure to environmental toxins, such as certain chemicals and industrial compounds, may also help minimize potential risks.
