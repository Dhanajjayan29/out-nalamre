Allergies

  • Nalamaree Team
  • 16 April 2024

Overview

Allergies can manifest in various ways and may have different treatments based on the specific allergy. It's important to note that allergies are immune system responses to substances that are usually harmless. Common allergens include pollen, dust mites, pet dander, certain foods, insect stings, and certain medications. Allergic reactions can range from mild to severe, and in some cases, they can be life-threatening.

Common allergens include pollen, dust mites, pet dander, certain foods (such as nuts, shellfish, dairy), insect stings, and certain medications. When a person with allergies encounters an allergen, their immune system may overreact.

Causes

  • Hay Fever (Allergic Rhinitis): This type of allergy occurs when the immune system overreacts to airborne allergens such as pollen, dust mites, pet dander, and mold spores. Symptoms include sneezing, runny or stuffy nose, itchy eyes, nose, and throat, and congestion.
  • Food Allergies: Food allergies occur when the immune system identifies a specific food or ingredient as harmful. Common food allergens include peanuts, tree nuts, eggs, milk, wheat, soy, fish, and shellfish. Symptoms can range from mild itching and hives to severe reactions such as swelling, difficulty breathing, and anaphylaxis.
  • Drug Allergies: Some people may be allergic to certain medications, such as penicillin, aspirin, nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), and antibiotics. Symptoms of drug allergies can vary but may include skin rash, hives, itching, swelling, wheezing, and anaphylaxis
  • Insect Sting Allergies: Allergic reactions to insect stings from bees, wasps, hornets, and fire ants can cause localized swelling, redness, and pain at the sting site. In some cases, people may experience severe reactions such as difficulty breathing, hives, swelling of the face or throat, dizziness, and anaphylaxis.
  • Latex Allergy: Latex allergies occur in response to proteins found in natural rubber latex, which is commonly used in gloves, condoms, balloons, and medical devices. Symptoms can range from skin irritation and hives to more severe reactions such as difficulty breathing and anaphylaxis.
  • Allergic Conjunctivitis: This type of allergy affects the eyes and is commonly triggered by allergens such as pollen, pet dander, dust mites, and mold spores. Symptoms include red, itchy, watery eyes, swelling, and a gritty feeling in the eyes.
  • Contact Dermatitis: Contact dermatitis is an allergic reaction that occurs when the skin comes into contact with certain substances, such as metals (e.g., nickel), cosmetics, fragrances, and latex. Symptoms include redness, itching, swelling, and blistering of the skin at the contact site.

    • Symptoms

  • Sneezing: Allergens such as pollen, dust, or pet dander can irritate the nasal passages, leading to frequent sneezing.
  • Runny or Stuffy Nose: Allergic reactions can cause inflammation in the nasal passages, resulting in a runny or congested nose.
  • Itchy or Watery Eyes: Irritants like pollen or pet dander can trigger an allergic response in the eyes, causing itching, redness, and excessive tearing.
  • Skin Rash: Allergic reactions may manifest as skin rashes, such as hives (raised, red, itchy welts on the skin) or eczema (dry, red, and itchy patches of skin).
  • Swelling: In some cases, allergies can cause swelling, particularly around the eyes, lips, face, or throat.
  • Coughing or Wheezing: Allergens can irritate the respiratory tract, leading to symptoms such as coughing, wheezing, or difficulty breathing, especially in individuals with asthma.
  • Sore Throat: Postnasal drip resulting from allergic reactions can lead to a sore or scratchy throat.
  • Fatigue: Chronic allergies can cause fatigue and affect overall energy levels, especially if symptoms disrupt sleep.
  • Headache: Some individuals may experience headaches as a result of sinus congestion or pressure caused by allergic reactions.
  • Digestive Symptoms: Food allergies can lead to gastrointestinal symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, or diarrhea.

    • Treatment: Modern Medicine

  • Antihistamines: These medications help block the effects of histamine, a chemical released during an allergic reaction. They can relieve symptoms such as itching, sneezing, and runny nose.
  • Decongestants: These can help reduce nasal congestion by narrowing blood vessels in the nasal passages.
  • Corticosteroids: Nasal sprays or oral medications containing corticosteroids can reduce inflammation and relieve symptoms.
  • Leukotriene modifiers: These medications block the action of certain immune system chemicals and can help relieve allergy symptoms.
  • Immunotherapy (Allergy Shots): In some cases, allergen-specific immunotherapy may be recommended. This involves gradually exposing the individual to increasing amounts of the allergen to desensitize the immune system.

    • Treatment: Traditional Medicine

  • Saline Nasal Rinse: Using a saline solution to rinse the nasal passages can help clear out allergens and irritants, reduce nasal congestion, and relieve sinus pressure. You can use a saline nasal spray or a neti pot for this purpose.

  • Local Honey: Some people believe that consuming locally produced honey may help desensitize the body to pollen allergens, although scientific evidence supporting this claim is limited. While honey can be a delicious addition to your diet, it's essential to note that it may not provide significant relief for everyone.

  • Steam Inhalation: Inhaling steam from a bowl of hot water or taking a hot shower can help relieve nasal congestion and soothe irritated nasal passages. Adding essential oils like eucalyptus or peppermint to the steam may provide additional benefits.

  • Quercetin: Quercetin is a plant pigment with antioxidant properties that may help reduce allergy symptoms by stabilizing mast cells and inhibiting the release of histamine. Foods rich in quercetin include apples, onions, berries, citrus fruits, red grapes, and leafy greens. Quercetin supplements are also available, but it's important to consult a healthcare provider before taking them, especially if you have any existing medical conditions or are taking medications.

  • Butterbur: Butterbur is a herb that has been used traditionally to treat allergies and migraines. Some studies suggest that butterbur extract may help relieve allergic rhinitis symptoms by reducing inflammation and blocking the effects of histamine. However, it's essential to choose a standardized butterbur extract and consult a healthcare professional before using it, as it may have side effects and interactions with certain medications.

  • Probiotics: Some research suggests that probiotics, which are beneficial bacteria that promote gut health, may help modulate the immune system and reduce allergy symptoms. Consuming probiotic-rich foods like yogurt, kefir, kimchi, and sauerkraut or taking probiotic supplements may be beneficial for some individuals with allergies.

  • Avoidance of Allergens: One of the most effective ways to manage allergies is to minimize exposure to allergens. This may involve using allergen-proof covers on pillows and mattresses, keeping windows closed during high pollen seasons, using air purifiers with HEPA filters, and regularly vacuuming and dusting your home.

    • Caution

  • Food: Common allergens include nuts, dairy, eggs, shellfish, and wheat.
  • Environmental: Pollen, dust mites, animal dander, and mold are common environmental allergens.
  • Medications: Some people are allergic to certain medications.

    • Prevention

  • Identify Allergens: Work with a healthcare professional to identify specific allergens triggering your symptoms through allergy testing. This may involve skin prick tests, blood tests, or elimination diets for food allergies.
  • Avoid Allergens: Once you know your triggers, take steps to minimize exposure. For example, if you're allergic to pollen, stay indoors on high pollen days or use air purifiers with HEPA filters. If you have food allergies, read ingredient labels carefully and avoid cross-contamination.
  • Keep Indoor Environments Clean: Reduce indoor allergens such as dust mites, pet dander, and mold by regularly vacuuming, dusting, and using allergen-proof covers on pillows and mattresses. Keep humidity levels low to prevent mold growth.
  • Practice Good Hygiene: Wash your hands frequently, especially after outdoor activities, to remove allergens like pollen. Shower and change clothes after being outside during high pollen seasons.
  • Manage Pet Allergies: If you're allergic to pets, consider limiting exposure by keeping pets out of certain areas of the home, using HEPA air purifiers, and bathing pets regularly.
  • Control Outdoor Allergens: Monitor pollen counts and try to limit outdoor activities on high pollen days. Wear sunglasses and hats to protect your eyes and face from pollen exposure.
