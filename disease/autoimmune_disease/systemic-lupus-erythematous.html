Systemic Lupus Erythematous

  • Nalamaree Team
  • 27 April 2024

Overview

Systemic Lupus Erythematosus, commonly referred to as lupus, is an autoimmune disease where the immune system mistakenly attacks healthy tissues in various parts of the body. This can lead to inflammation and damage to different organs, such as the skin, joints, kidneys, heart, lungs, brain, and blood vessels.

Causes

  • Immune System Dysfunction: Lupus is primarily characterized by dysregulation of the immune system, leading to the production of autoantibodies that target healthy tissues. It is believed that a combination of genetic predisposition and environmental triggers disrupts the immune system's normal function, causing it to mistakenly attack the body's own cells and tissues.
  • Genetic Factors: Genetics play a role in predisposing individuals to lupus. People with a family history of lupus or other autoimmune diseases are at higher risk. Certain genetic variations may affect how the immune system functions, increasing susceptibility to autoimmune reactions.
  • Environmental Triggers: Various environmental factors can trigger or exacerbate lupus symptoms in susceptible individuals. These triggers may include infections (such as Epstein-Barr virus), exposure to ultraviolet light from sunlight, certain medications (such as hydralazine, procainamide, and others), and exposure to certain chemicals.
  • Hormonal Factors: Hormonal fluctuations, particularly in women, are thought to contribute to the development and exacerbation of lupus. Women are more commonly affected by lupus than men, and the onset or worsening of symptoms often occurs during periods of hormonal change, such as puberty, pregnancy, or menopause.

    • Symptoms

    Common symptoms of lupus include:

  • Fatigue: Persistent and overwhelming tiredness that is not relieved by rest.
  • Joint Pain and Swelling: Pain, stiffness, and swelling in the joints, often affecting multiple joints simultaneously.
  • Skin Rashes: Various types of skin rashes may occur, with the most characteristic being a butterfly-shaped rash across the cheeks and bridge of the nose. Other rashes may appear on the face, neck, arms, or other parts of the body, often in response to sunlight.
  • Fever: Low-grade fever or recurrent fevers.
  • Photosensitivity: Increased sensitivity to sunlight or artificial ultraviolet (UV) light, which can trigger or worsen skin rashes and other symptoms.
  • Oral and Nasal Ulcers: Painful sores or ulcers in the mouth, nose, or throat.
  • Raynaud's Phenomenon: Cold-induced color changes in the fingers and toes, such as turning white or blue in response to cold or stress.
  • Hair Loss: Thinning of the hair or hair loss, often in patches.
  • Chest Pain: Chest pain may occur due to inflammation of the lining surrounding the heart (pericarditis) or other causes.
  • Shortness of Breath: Difficulty breathing or shortness of breath, which may result from inflammation in the lungs or other complications.
  • Kidney Problems: Lupus nephritis, inflammation of the kidneys, can cause symptoms such as swelling in the legs, high blood pressure, and changes in urinary habits.
  • Neurological Symptoms: Headaches, seizures, confusion, memory loss, and other neurological symptoms may occur if lupus affects the central nervous system.
  • Gastrointestinal Symptoms: Abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea may occur, sometimes due to inflammation of the gastrointestinal tract.

    • Treatment: Modern Medicine

    Here are some common treatment methods used in modern medicine for lupus:

  • Nonsteroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs (NSAIDs): NSAIDs such as ibuprofen or naproxen can help relieve mild to moderate joint pain, stiffness, and inflammation associated with lupus.
  • Antimalarial Drugs: Hydroxychloroquine (Plaquenil) is commonly prescribed to manage fatigue, joint pain, skin rashes, and other lupus symptoms. It also helps prevent flares and may have protective effects on the heart and kidneys.
  • Corticosteroids: Prednisone and other corticosteroids are often used to reduce inflammation and suppress the immune system during lupus flares. However, long-term use of corticosteroids can cause serious side effects, so they are typically used at the lowest effective dose for the shortest duration possible.
  • Immunosuppressant Medications: Drugs such as azathioprine (Imuran), mycophenolate mofetil (CellCept), methotrexate, and cyclophosphamide may be prescribed to suppress the immune system and reduce inflammation in people with severe lupus or organ-threatening complications.
  • Biologic Therapies: Biologic drugs, such as belimumab (Benlysta), specifically target components of the immune system involved in lupus. Belimumab is the first biologic therapy approved for the treatment of lupus and is used in certain cases where other treatments have not been effective.
  •  Treatments: Topical corticosteroids or calcineurin inhibitors may be prescribed to relieve skin rashes and lesions associated with lupus.

    • Treatment: Traditional Medicine

  • Dietary Modifications: Eating a well-balanced diet rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, lean proteins, and healthy fats may help support overall health and reduce inflammation. Some people with lupus may benefit from avoiding certain foods that can trigger symptoms or exacerbate inflammation, such as processed foods, excessive salt, and saturated fats.
  • Omega-3 Fatty Acids: Foods rich in omega-3 fatty acids, such as fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines), flaxseeds, chia seeds, and walnuts, may have anti-inflammatory properties and may help reduce inflammation associated with lupus.
  • Turmeric: Turmeric, a spice commonly used in traditional medicine, contains curcumin, which has anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties. Adding turmeric to food or taking turmeric supplements may help reduce inflammation and alleviate joint pain in some individuals with lupus.
  • Ginger: Ginger is another herb with anti-inflammatory properties that may help reduce pain and inflammation associated with lupus. Ginger can be consumed fresh, as a tea, or in supplement form.
  • Green Tea: Green tea contains polyphenols and antioxidants that may help reduce inflammation and support overall health. Drinking green tea regularly may provide some benefits for individuals with lupus, but it's essential to monitor caffeine intake and consult with a healthcare provider, as excessive caffeine consumption may exacerbate certain symptoms.
  • Heat and Cold Therapy: Applying heat or cold packs to affected joints or muscles may help reduce pain and stiffness associated with lupus flares. Experiment with both heat and cold therapy to determine which works best for you.

    • Caution

  • Monitor for Side Effects: Be vigilant for any adverse reactions or side effects while using traditional or home remedies. If you experience any new or worsening symptoms, stop using the remedy and consult with your healthcare provider.
  • Use Moderation: When using herbal supplements or other natural remedies, use moderation and follow recommended dosages. Taking excessive amounts of certain supplements may lead to adverse effects or toxicity.
  • Complementary, Not Alternative: While traditional and home remedies can complement medical treatment for lupus, they should not be viewed as alternatives to conventional medical care. Medical treatment prescribed by healthcare professionals remains the cornerstone of managing lupus and preventing complications.
  • Monitor Disease Activity: Continue to monitor disease activity and symptoms closely, even when using traditional or home remedies. Regular follow-up with your healthcare provider is essential for assessing treatment effectiveness, adjusting medications, and addressing any concerns.

    • Prevention

  • Maintain a Healthy Lifestyle: Adopting healthy habits such as eating a balanced diet, engaging in regular physical activity, maintaining a healthy weight, and avoiding smoking may help support overall health and immune function.
  • Sun Protection: Limit exposure to sunlight and ultraviolet (UV) light, as sunlight can trigger lupus flares and exacerbate skin symptoms. Wear protective clothing, use broad-spectrum sunscreen with a high SPF, and seek shade when outdoors, especially during peak sun hours.
  • Manage Stress: Stress can trigger lupus flares and worsen symptoms. Practice stress management techniques such as relaxation exercises, mindfulness meditation, yoga, or deep breathing exercises to reduce stress and promote emotional well-being.
  • Limit Exposure to Environmental Triggers: Be mindful of potential environmental triggers such as certain medications, chemicals, and infections. Avoid known triggers whenever possible, and take precautions to minimize exposure.
  • Monitor Hormonal Changes: Fluctuations in hormone levels, such as those occurring during puberty, pregnancy, or menopause, may influence lupus activity. Consult with your healthcare provider about strategies for managing hormonal changes and monitoring lupus symptoms during these periods.
  • Know Your Family History: If you have a family history of lupus or other autoimmune diseases, make sure to inform your healthcare provider. While you cannot change your genetic predisposition, being aware of your family history can help guide screening and preventive measures.
