Psoriasis

Overview

Psoriasis is a chronic autoimmune skin condition characterized by rapid buildup of skin cells, leading to scales and red patches. It can cause itching, soreness, and pain. Common areas affected include the elbows, knees, scalp, and lower back.

Types of Psoriasis:

  • Plaque Psoriasis: Raised red patches covered with silvery-white scales.
  • Guttate Psoriasis: Drop-like lesions, often triggered by infections.
  • Inverse Psoriasis: Smooth, red patches in skin folds.
  • Pustular Psoriasis: White pus-filled blisters on red skin.
  • Erythrodermic Psoriasis: Severe redness and scaling across large areas.

Causes

  • Genetic Predisposition: Family history can increase risk.
  • Immune System Dysfunction: Overactive T-cells cause inflammation and rapid skin cell turnover.
  • Environmental Triggers: Infections, stress, skin injuries, and certain medications can exacerbate symptoms.
  • Lifestyle Factors: Smoking, obesity, and alcohol can worsen symptoms.
  • Hormonal Changes: Puberty, pregnancy, and menopause may influence symptoms.

Symptoms

  • Red patches with silvery scales
  • Dry, cracked skin that may bleed
  • Itching, burning, or soreness
  • Thickened, pitted, or ridged nails
  • Joint pain or swelling (psoriatic arthritis)

Treatment: Modern Medicine

  • Topical Treatments: Corticosteroids, vitamin D analogs, and retinoids.
  • Phototherapy: UV light exposure under medical supervision.
  • Systemic Medications: Oral retinoids, methotrexate, and biologics.
  • Biologic Therapies: Target specific immune system components.
  • Oral Medications: Apremilast for psoriasis and psoriatic arthritis.

Treatment: Traditional Medicine

  • Home Remedies: Aloe vera, coconut oil, and jojoba oil for relief.
  • Oatmeal Baths: Reduces itching and inflammation.
  • Probiotics: Beneficial for immune modulation.
  • Omega-3 Fatty Acids: Anti-inflammatory effects from fish and seeds.

Caution

  • Side Effects: Discuss potential risks with a healthcare provider.
  • Monitoring: Regular check-ups may be required.
  • Pregnancy and Breastfeeding: Consult your doctor before starting treatments.
  • Immunosuppression: Increased infection risk with some treatments.
  • Sun Sensitivity: Avoid excessive sun exposure during treatment.
  • Drug Interactions: Inform your provider about all medications.

Prevention

  • Maintain a Healthy Lifestyle: Balanced diet and regular exercise.
  • Manage Stress: Techniques like meditation may help.
  • Avoid Triggers: Identify and prevent factors that worsen symptoms.
  • Good Skin Hygiene: Use mild soaps and moisturizers.
  • Protect Your Skin: Sunscreen and protective clothing are advised.
  • Follow Treatment Plans: Regular monitoring helps manage the condition.

    Psoriasis

    • Nalamaree Team
    • 16 April 2024

    Overview

    Psoriasis is a chronic autoimmune skin condition characterized by the rapid buildup of skin cells. This leads to the formation of scales and red patches, often accompanied by itching, soreness, and sometimes pain. Psoriasis can occur on any part of the body but is most commonly found on the elbows, knees, scalp, lower back, and nails.

    Types of Psoriasis:

  • Plaque Psoriasis: The most common form, characterized by raised, red patches covered with a silvery-white buildup of dead skin cells.
  • Guttate Psoriasis: Small, drop-like lesions often triggered by bacterial infections.
  • Inverse Psoriasis: Smooth, red patches in skin folds, such as under the breasts, in the armpits, or around the genitals.
  • Pustular Psoriasis: White, pus-filled blisters surrounded by red skin.
  • Erythrodermic Psoriasis: Widespread redness, severe itching, and shedding of scales in sheets. This is a rare but serious form of psoriasis.

    • Causes

    Psoriasis is a chronic autoimmune disease that affects the skin and sometimes the joints. It causes skin cells to grow too quickly, resulting in thick, scaly patches that can be red, silver, or white. Psoriasis is not contagious and it can affect people of all ages, although it most commonly starts between the ages of 15 and 35.

  • ﻿Genetic predisposition: Psoriasis tends to run in families, suggesting a genetic component. Certain genetic variations may increase the risk of developing psoriasis.
  • Immune system dysfunction: Psoriasis is considered an autoimmune disease, meaning the immune system mistakenly attacks healthy cells in the body. In psoriasis, immune cells called T cells become overactive and trigger inflammation, leading to the rapid turnover of skin cells.
  • Environmental triggers: Various environmental factors can exacerbate or trigger psoriasis symptoms in susceptible individuals. These triggers may include infections (such as streptococcal infections), stress, injury to the skin (such as cuts or sunburns), certain medications (such as lithium, beta-blockers, and antimalarial drugs), and smoking.
  • Lifestyle factors: Lifestyle choices such as smoking, obesity, excessive alcohol consumption, and a poor diet may contribute to the development or worsening of psoriasis symptoms in some individuals.
  • Hormonal changes: Fluctuations in hormone levels, such as those that occur during puberty, pregnancy, or menopause, can influence psoriasis symptoms in some individuals.

    • Symptoms

  • Psoriasis is a chronic autoimmune condition that primarily affects the skin, leading to the development of red, scaly patches or plaques.
  • Red patches of skin covered with thick, silvery scales (plaques)
  • Dry, cracked skin that may bleed
  • Itching, burning, or soreness in the affected areas
  • Thickened, pitted, or ridged nails
  • Joint pain or swelling (psoriatic arthritis).

    • Psoriasis can occur anywhere on the body but is commonly found on the scalp, elbows, knees, and lower back.

    Treatment: Modern Medicine

  • Topical Treatments: These are applied directly to the skin and are often the first line of treatment for mild to moderate psoriasis. They include corticosteroids, vitamin D Analogs, Retinoids,  coal tar, and calcineurin inhibitors.
  • Phototherapy (Light Therapy): This involves exposing the skin to ultraviolet (UV) light under medical supervision. Phototherapy can be done with natural sunlight or artificial light sources such as UVB lamps.
  • Systemic Medications: These are prescribed for moderate to severe psoriasis or when topical treatments and phototherapy are ineffective. Systemic medications include oral Retinoids, Methotrexate, Cyclosporine, and biologics (such as TNF-alpha inhibitors, IL-17 inhibitors, and IL-23 inhibitors).
  • Biologic Therapies: Biologics are a type of systemic medication that target specific parts of the immune system involved in the development of psoriasis. They are usually reserved for severe cases or when other treatments have failed.
  • Oral Medications: In addition to biologics, oral medications such as Apremilast may be prescribed for the treatment of psoriasis and psoriatic arthritis.

    • Treatment: Traditional Medicine

  • Home Remedies: Natural topical treatments such as aloe vera, coconut oil, olive oil, and jojoba oil may help soothe inflamed skin and reduce scaling and redness. Applying these oils or gels directly to the affected areas can provide relief.
  • Oatmeal Baths: Taking oatmeal baths can help relieve itching and soothe irritated skin. Adding colloidal oatmeal or finely ground oatmeal to lukewarm bathwater and soaking for 15-20 minutes can help hydrate the skin and reduce inflammation.
  • Probiotics: Probiotics, which are beneficial bacteria found in fermented foods like yogurt, kefir, and sauerkraut, may help modulate the immune system and reduce inflammation associated with psoriasis. Adding probiotic-rich foods to your diet or taking probiotic supplements may be beneficial.
  • Omega-3 Fatty Acids: Omega-3 fatty acids found in fatty fish, flaxseeds, chia seeds, and walnuts have anti-inflammatory properties and may help reduce inflammation and improve symptoms of psoriasis. Adding these foods to your diet or taking omega-3 supplements may be helpful.

    • Caution

  • Side Effects: Many psoriasis treatments, especially systemic medications and biologics, can have side effects. It's important to discuss potential side effects with your healthcare provider before starting any treatment and to report any adverse reactions promptly.
  • Monitoring: Some treatments may require regular monitoring of blood tests or other parameters to monitor for potential side effects or complications. Make sure to follow up with your healthcare provider as recommended.
  • Pregnancy and Breastfeeding: Some psoriasis medications may not be safe to use during pregnancy or while breastfeeding. If you are pregnant, planning to become pregnant, or breastfeeding, discuss treatment options with your healthcare provider to ensure the safety of both you and your baby.
  • Immunosuppression: Some systemic medications and biologics used to treat psoriasis work by suppressing the immune system. This can increase the risk of infections or other immune-related complications. Practice good hygiene and discuss any concerns with your healthcare provider.
  • Sun Sensitivity: Certain psoriasis treatments, such as phototherapy and some topical medications, can increase sensitivity to sunlight. Take precautions to protect your skin from excessive sun exposure, such as wearing sunscreen and protective clothing.
  • Drug Interactions: Some psoriasis medications may interact with other medications or supplements you are taking. Make sure to inform your healthcare provider about all medications, supplements, and over-the-counter products you are using to avoid potential interactions.
  • Long-Term Use: Some treatments for psoriasis may not be suitable for long-term use due to potential side effects or diminishing effectiveness over time. 

    • Prevention

  • Maintain a Healthy Lifestyle: Eating a balanced diet, exercising regularly, maintaining a healthy weight, and avoiding smoking and excessive alcohol consumption can help support overall health and potentially reduce the severity of psoriasis symptoms.
  • Manage Stress: Stress is known to trigger or exacerbate psoriasis flare-ups in some individuals. Practicing stress-reduction techniques such as mindfulness, meditation, yoga, or deep breathing exercises may help manage stress levels and reduce the frequency or severity of flare-ups.
  • Avoid Triggers: Identifying and avoiding potential triggers that worsen psoriasis symptoms can help prevent flare-ups. Common triggers include stress, infections, injuries to the skin, certain medications, and environmental factors such as cold weather or dry air.
  • Maintain Good Skin Hygiene: Taking good care of your skin can help minimize irritation and reduce the risk of flare-ups. This includes using mild, fragrance-free soaps and moisturizers, avoiding harsh chemicals or irritants, and gently patting (rather than rubbing) the skin dry after bathing.
  • Protect Your Skin: Protecting your skin from injury and excessive sun exposure can help prevent flare-ups and reduce the risk of developing new lesions. Use sunscreen with a high SPF, wear protective clothing and accessories (such as hats and sunglasses), and take precautions to avoid cuts, scrapes, or other skin injuries.
  • Follow Treatment Plans: If you have been diagnosed with psoriasis, following your prescribed treatment plan and regularly monitoring your symptoms can help manage the condition and prevent flare-ups.
