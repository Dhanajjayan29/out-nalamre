Rheumatoid Arthritis

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is an autoimmune disease that causes chronic inflammation in the joints. Unlike osteoarthritis, which results from wear and tear of cartilage, rheumatoid arthritis is a systemic condition where the immune system attacks the synovium (lining of the joints), leading to pain, swelling, and potential joint damage. It can also affect other organs in the body, such as the heart, lungs, and eyes.

Causes and Risk Factors

The exact cause of rheumatoid arthritis is unknown, but it is thought to be a combination of genetic and environmental factors. Common risk factors include:

  • Age: RA is most commonly diagnosed in people between 40 and 60 years of age.
  • Gender: Women are more likely to develop RA than men.
  • Family history: Having a family member with RA increases the risk.
  • Smoking: Smoking is a major environmental risk factor for RA.

Symptoms

The symptoms of rheumatoid arthritis can vary from mild to severe. Common symptoms include:

  • Joint pain, especially in the hands, wrists, and knees.
  • Swelling and stiffness in the joints, particularly in the morning or after periods of inactivity.
  • Fatigue and weakness.
  • Loss of appetite and fever.
  • Rheumatoid nodules, which are firm lumps under the skin near affected joints.

Treatment Options

Treatment for rheumatoid arthritis focuses on reducing inflammation, relieving pain, and preventing joint damage. Options include:

Medications:
  • Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) to relieve pain and inflammation.
  • Disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs (DMARDs) to slow the progression of RA.
  • Biologics, which are newer drugs that target specific parts of the immune system.
Surgical Interventions:
  • Joint replacement surgery for severe cases of RA.
  • Synovectomy to remove the inflamed joint lining.
Lifestyle Changes:
  • Physical therapy to improve joint function and flexibility.
  • Regular exercise to maintain joint mobility and reduce stiffness.

Prevention Tips

Although rheumatoid arthritis cannot be prevented, you can take steps to reduce your risk:

  • Quit smoking to lower your risk of developing RA.
  • Maintain a healthy weight to reduce stress on your joints.
  • Eat a balanced diet rich in anti-inflammatory foods, such as omega-3 fatty acids.
