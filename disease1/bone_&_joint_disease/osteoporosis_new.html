Osteoporosis

Osteoporosis is a condition that causes bones to become weak and brittle, increasing the risk of fractures. The condition often progresses without noticeable symptoms until a fracture occurs. It is especially common in older adults and postmenopausal women.

Causes and Risk Factors

Osteoporosis develops when the body either loses too much bone mass or does not produce enough new bone tissue. Several factors contribute to the development of osteoporosis, including:

  • Age: Bone mass naturally decreases as you age.
  • Gender: Women are more likely to develop osteoporosis, especially postmenopausal women.
  • Family history: Genetics can play a role in the likelihood of developing osteoporosis.
  • Lack of physical activity: A sedentary lifestyle can weaken bones.
  • Nutrition: Poor diet, low calcium, and vitamin D intake can increase the risk.

Symptoms

Osteoporosis often progresses without any symptoms until a fracture occurs. However, common signs may include:

  • Back pain, which can result from a fractured or collapsed vertebra.
  • Loss of height over time.
  • A stooped posture.
  • Fractures that occur more easily than expected.

Treatment Options

The goal of osteoporosis treatment is to prevent bone fractures and improve bone strength. Treatment options include:

Medications:
  • Bisphosphonates to slow bone loss.
  • Hormone replacement therapy (HRT) for postmenopausal women.
  • Bone-building medications like teriparatide.
Lifestyle Changes:
  • Increasing calcium and vitamin D intake.
  • Regular weight-bearing exercises like walking or strength training.
  • Avoiding smoking and excessive alcohol consumption.

Prevention Tips

Preventing osteoporosis involves maintaining strong bones throughout life:

  • Consume a balanced diet with adequate calcium and vitamin D.
  • Engage in regular weight-bearing exercises.
  • Avoid smoking and limit alcohol intake.
