Insulin-Dependent Diabetes Mellitus (IDDM)

Insulin-dependent diabetes mellitus (IDDM), also known as Type 1 Diabetes, is a chronic condition where the pancreas produces little or no insulin. It typically occurs in childhood or early adulthood.

Causes and Risk Factors

The exact cause of IDDM is not fully understood, but it is believed to result from an autoimmune attack on the insulin-producing beta cells in the pancreas. The risk factors include:

  • Genetic predisposition.
  • Viral infections that may trigger the immune system to attack the pancreas.
  • Family history of Type 1 diabetes.
  • Environmental factors such as exposure to toxins or certain foods during infancy.

Risk factors for IDDM include:

  • A family history of Type 1 diabetes.
  • Having other autoimmune disorders.
  • Living in areas with lower exposure to sunlight.

Symptoms

The symptoms of IDDM can develop rapidly and may include:

  • Frequent urination.
  • Excessive thirst and hunger.
  • Unexplained weight loss.
  • Fatigue and weakness.
  • Blurred vision.
  • Slow healing of cuts and wounds.

Treatment Options

The primary treatment for IDDM is the administration of insulin, as the body can no longer produce it on its own. Common treatment options include:

Insulin Therapy:
  • Daily insulin injections or an insulin pump to manage blood sugar levels.
  • Types of insulin include rapid-acting, long-acting, and intermediate insulin.
Blood Sugar Monitoring:
  • Regular blood glucose testing to ensure blood sugar levels are within a safe range.
  • Continuous glucose monitors (CGMs) for real-time data on glucose levels.
Lifestyle Adjustments:
  • Balanced diet, regular exercise, and managing stress to keep blood sugar levels stable.

Prevention Tips

While IDDM cannot be prevented, managing the condition can reduce the risk of complications:

  • Maintaining a healthy weight and avoiding obesity.
  • Engaging in regular physical activity.
  • Monitoring blood sugar levels regularly.
  • Following prescribed insulin regimens and medical advice.

    IDDM-Insulin Dependent Diabetes Melitus(Type 1)

    • Nalamaree Team
    • 24 April 2024

    Overview

    IDDM, which stands for Insulin-Dependent Diabetes Mellitus, is an older term for what is now more commonly known as type 1 diabetes. It is a chronic autoimmune condition that affects the pancreas, an organ located behind the stomach. The pancreas produces several important hormones, including insulin, which helps regulate blood sugar levels.

    Causes

    In people with Type 1 diabetes, the immune system mistakenly attacks and destroys the insulin-producing beta cells in the pancreas. This leads to a deficiency of insulin, which means that the body cannot properly use glucose (sugar) for energy. As a result, blood sugar levels rise too high, leading to the symptoms of diabetes.

  • Genetic Predisposition: Genetics plays a significant role in the development of Type 1 diabetes. Individuals with a family history of the condition are at higher risk. .
  • Autoimmune Response: Type 1 diabetes is classified as an autoimmune disease because the body's immune system mistakenly targets and destroys its own cells. In the case of Type 1 diabetes, the immune system attacks and destroys the insulin-producing beta cells in the pancreas.
  • Environmental Factors: Various environmental factors have been proposed as potential triggers for the development of Type 1 diabetes. These factors may include viral infections (such as enteroviruses, mumps, rubella, and cytomegalovirus), dietary factors (such as early exposure to cow's milk or gluten), and environmental toxins. However, the specific role of these factors in triggering the autoimmune response in Type 1 diabetes is still under investigation.
  • Hygiene Hypothesis: The hygiene hypothesis suggests that reduced exposure to certain infections and microorganisms in early childhood may contribute to the development of autoimmune diseases like Type 1 diabetes. According to this hypothesis, a lack of exposure to infectious agents during early childhood may lead to an imbalance in the development of the immune system, increasing the risk of autoimmune diseases.
  • Other Factors: Other factors that may contribute to the development of Type 1 diabetes include prenatal and perinatal factors (such as maternal infections during pregnancy), dietary factors (such as vitamin D deficiency), and the timing of the introduction of certain foods during infancy. .

    • Symptoms

    The symptoms of type 1 diabetes typically develop over a short period, ranging from days to weeks, though they can sometimes be subtle and progress slowly. 

    Classic "3 Ps":

  • Polydipsia: Excessive thirst is a cardinal symptom, as the body attempts to eliminate excess sugar through urination.
  • Polyuria: Increased urination, particularly at night (nocturia), is another hallmark sign as the body tries to expel unutilized glucose.
  • Polyphagia: Intense hunger is often present, as the cells are starved for energy due to the lack of insulin.

    • Additional Symptoms:

  • Unexplained weight loss: Despite increased hunger, the body loses muscle and fat due to the inability to efficiently use glucose for energy.
  • Fatigue: Constant tiredness and lack of energy are common consequences of the body's compromised energy production.
  • Blurred vision: High blood sugar levels can cause temporary fluctuations in the lens of the eye, leading to blurry vision.
  • Slow healing of cuts and sores: Impaired blood circulation and immune function can delay wound healing.
  • Fruity-smelling breath: A distinctive acetone odor on the breath can occur when the body breaks down fat for energy due to insulin deficiency.
  • Irritability and mood swings: Fluctuations in blood sugar levels can affect mood and behavior.
  • Hypoglycemia: Low blood sugar can cause fatigue, dizziness, headache, and nausea. In severe cases, it can lead to seizures or loss of consciousness.
  • Hyperglycemia: High blood sugar levels can cause damage to organs, particularly the kidneys and eyes, leading to long-term complications such as blind

    • Treatment: Modern Medicine

  • Insulin Therapy: Since the body cannot produce its own insulin, the primary treatment for type 1 diabetes is insulin replacement therapy. This involves administering insulin through various methods, including:
  • Injections: Multiple daily injections using vials and syringes, pens, or insulin pumps.
  • Insulin pumps: These small, programmable devices deliver insulin continuously throughout the day, with adjustments for meals and activity.
  • SGLT2 inhibitors: These medications help your kidneys remove excess sugar from your blood through urine , Dapagliflozin (Farxiga), Canagliflozin (Canagliflozin), Empagliflozin (Jardiance).

    • Treatment: Traditional Medicine

  • Cinnamon: Some studies suggest that cinnamon may help improve insulin sensitivity and lower blood sugar levels.
  • Gymnema sylvestre: This herb is traditionally used in Ayurvedic medicine for diabetes management and may help lower blood sugar levels.
  • Bitter melon: Bitter melon has been studied for its potential antidiabetic effects, although more research is needed to confirm its efficacy.
  • Apple Cider Vinegar: Some people believe that apple cider vinegar may help improve insulin sensitivity and lower blood sugar levels. It can be consumed diluted in water before meals, but it's essential to monitor blood sugar levels closely, as vinegar can affect blood sugar levels.
  • Aloe Vera: Some studies suggest that aloe vera gel may have antidiabetic effects and help improve blood sugar control.
  • Fenugreek Seeds: Fenugreek seeds have been studied for their potential antidiabetic effects. Some research suggests that fenugreek seeds may help improve insulin sensitivity and lower blood sugar levels. Fenugreek seeds can be soaked overnight and consumed in the morning, or they can be added to dishes for flavor and potential health benefits.

    • Caution

  • Foot Care: Individuals with Type 1 diabetes are at increased risk of foot problems due to nerve damage (neuropathy) and reduced blood flow (peripheral vascular disease). Proper foot care, including daily inspection, keeping feet clean and dry, wearing comfortable and supportive footwear, and avoiding walking barefoot, is important to prevent complications such as ulcers and infections.
  • Hypoglycemia Awareness: Hypoglycemia (low blood sugar) can occur in individuals with Type 1 diabetes, especially if insulin doses are too high or meals are delayed or skipped. 

    • Prevention

  • Blood Sugar Control: Maintaining optimal blood sugar control is crucial for preventing complications associated with Type 1 diabetes, such as cardiovascular disease, neuropathy, nephropathy, and retinopathy. Monitoring blood sugar levels regularly, adhering to prescribed insulin therapy, and making dietary and lifestyle modifications to keep blood sugar levels within the target range can help prevent long-term complications.
  • Healthy Lifestyle: Adopting a healthy lifestyle can help improve overall health and well-being for individuals with Type 1 diabetes. This includes eating a balanced diet that includes fruits, vegetables, whole grains, lean proteins, and healthy fats, as well as regular physical activity, adequate sleep, stress management, and avoiding tobacco and excessive alcohol consumption.
  • Regular Medical Check-ups: Regular medical check-ups and screenings are essential for individuals with Type 1 diabetes to monitor blood sugar control, assess for complications, and adjust treatment as needed. This may include regular visits to healthcare providers, eye exams, kidney function tests, and foot exams to prevent or detect complications early.
  • Education and Self-management: Education and self-management are key components of diabetes care. It's important for individuals with Type 1 diabetes and their caregivers to stay informed about diabetes management strategies, including insulin therapy, blood sugar monitoring, dietary management, physical activity, and medication adherence.
