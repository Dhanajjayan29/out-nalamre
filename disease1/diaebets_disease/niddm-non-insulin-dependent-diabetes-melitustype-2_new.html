    NIDDM-Non Insulin Dependent Diabetes Melitus(Type 2)

    • Nalamaree Team
    • 24 April 2024

    Overview

    NIDDM stands for Non-Insulin Dependent Diabetes Mellitus, which is also commonly known as Type 2 diabetes. This form of diabetes is characterized by insulin resistance, meaning that the body's cells do not respond effectively to insulin, a hormone produced by the pancreas that helps regulate blood sugar levels. As a result, glucose builds up in the bloodstream instead of being absorbed into cells for energy, leading to high blood sugar levels.

    Causes

  • Genetics: Family history and genetics play a significant role in Type 2 diabetes risk. Individuals with a family history of diabetes are at higher risk themselves.
  • Insulin Resistance: Insulin resistance occurs when the body's cells don't respond properly to insulin. This means the body needs more insulin to help glucose enter cells for energy. Over time, the pancreas may struggle to produce enough insulin to compensate, leading to high blood sugar levels.
  • Obesity and Sedentary Lifestyle: Being overweight or obese increases the risk of developing insulin resistance and Type 2 diabetes. Physical inactivity exacerbates this risk. Fat tissue, particularly around the abdomen, releases substances that can contribute to insulin resistance.
  • Unhealthy Diet: A diet high in processed foods, sugars, and unhealthy fats, combined with low intake of fruits, vegetables, and whole grains, can contribute to obesity and insulin resistance.
  • Ethnicity: Certain ethnic groups, including African Americans, Hispanic/Latino Americans, Native Americans, Asian Americans, and Pacific Islanders, have a higher risk of developing Type 2 diabetes.
  • Gestational Diabetes: Women who have had gestational diabetes during pregnancy are at increased risk of developing Type 2 diabetes later in life.
  • Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS): Women with PCOS, a hormonal disorder, are at higher risk of developing insulin resistance and Type 2 diabetes.
  • Sleep Disorders: Conditions such as sleep apnea have been linked to insulin resistance and an increased risk of Type 2 diabetes.

    • Symptoms

  • Frequent Urination: Increased urination, especially during the night (nocturia), is a common symptom. This occurs as the body tries to eliminate excess sugar through urine.
  • Increased Thirst: Excessive thirst (polydipsia) often accompanies frequent urination, as the body tries to replenish lost fluids.
  • Increased Hunger: Despite eating, individuals with Type 2 diabetes may experience persistent hunger (polyphagia) due to cells not getting enough glucose for energy.
  • Unexplained Weight Loss: Some people may experience unexplained weight loss despite eating well. This can occur as the body breaks down fat and muscle tissue for energy because cells aren't getting enough glucose.
  • Fatigue: Feelings of fatigue and weakness may occur due to cells not receiving enough glucose for energy production.
  • Blurred Vision: High blood sugar levels can cause fluid to be pulled from the lenses of the eyes, affecting their ability to focus properly, leading to blurred vision.
  • Slow Wound Healing: High blood sugar levels can impair the body's ability to heal wounds, cuts, or sores.
  • Numbness or Tingling in Hands and Feet: Elevated blood sugar levels can damage nerves, leading to peripheral neuropathy, causing numbness, tingling, or pain in the hands and feet.
  • Recurrent Infections: Type 2 diabetes can weaken the immune system, making individuals more susceptible to infections, especially yeast infections in the genitals or frequent urinary tract infections.
  • Darkened Skin Patches: Some people with Type 2 diabetes may develop darkened patches of skin, especially in areas where the skin folds, such as the neck, armpits, or groin. This condition is called acanthosis nigricans and is often a sign of insulin resistance.

    • Treatment: Modern Medicine

  • Metformin: This is usually the first-line medication for Type 2 diabetes. Metformin works by reducing glucose production in the liver and improving insulin sensitivity in muscle tissue.
  • Sulfonylureas: These medications stimulate the pancreas to release more insulin. Examples include Glipizide, Glyburide, and Glimepiride.
  • DPP-4 Inhibitors: These drugs help lower blood sugar levels by blocking the enzyme dipeptidyl peptidase-4 (DPP-4), which breaks down incretin hormones that stimulate insulin release. Examples include Sitagliptin, Saxagliptin, and Linagliptin.
  • SGLT2 Inhibitors: These medications lower blood sugar levels by causing the kidneys to remove sugar from the body through urine. Examples include Canagliflozin, Dapagliflozin, and Empagliflozin.
  • GLP-1 Receptor Agonists: These drugs mimic the action of incretin hormones, which stimulate insulin release and reduce glucagon secretion. They also slow gastric emptying and increase satiety, leading to weight loss. Examples include Liraglutide, Exenatide, and Dulaglutide.
  • Insulin Therapy: Some individuals with Type 2 diabetes may eventually require insulin therapy to control blood sugar levels. Insulin can be administered through injections or insulin pump therapy. There are different types of insulin, including rapid-acting, short-acting, intermediate-acting, and long-acting, which may be used alone or in combination depending on individual needs.

    • Treatment: Traditional Medicine

    Dietary Modifications:

  • Herbal Teas: Some herbal teas, such as green tea, chamomile tea, and cinnamon tea, may have properties that could help improve blood sugar control. However, research on their effectiveness is limited, and they should not replace medical treatment.
  • Fenugreek: Fenugreek seeds are rich in soluble fiber and may help lower blood sugar levels. They can be soaked overnight and consumed in the morning or added to meals.
  • Bitter Melon: Bitter melon, also known as bitter gourd or karela, is a vegetable that may have blood sugar-lowering properties. It can be consumed raw, cooked, or juiced.

    • Supplements:

  • Alpha-Lipoic Acid (ALA): ALA is an antioxidant that may help improve insulin sensitivity and reduce oxidative stress in people with Type 2 diabetes. However, more research is needed to confirm its effectiveness.
  • Berberine: Berberine is a compound found in several plants, including goldenseal and barberry. Some studies suggest that it may help lower blood sugar levels by increasing insulin sensitivity and reducing glucose production in the liver.
  • Cinnamon: Some studies have shown that cinnamon may help improve blood sugar control by enhancing insulin sensitivity. However, more research is needed to confirm its effectiveness, and large amounts of cinnamon can be harmful.

    • Caution

  • Medication Management: If you're taking medications for Type 2 diabetes, it's crucial to follow your healthcare provider's instructions closely. Always take medications as prescribed and never adjust dosages or stop medications without consulting your doctor. Abrupt changes in medication can lead to fluctuations in blood sugar levels and potential complications.
  • Healthy Diet: While dietary modifications can be beneficial for managing blood sugar levels, it's important to approach dietary changes cautiously. Avoid extreme or restrictive diets that may lead to nutrient deficiencies or imbalances. Aim for a balanced diet that includes a variety of nutrient-rich foods, and work with a registered dietitian if necessary to develop a personalized meal plan.
  • Supplements and Herbal Remedies: While some supplements and herbal remedies may have potential benefits for managing Type 2 diabetes, it's important to use caution and consult with your healthcare provider before trying any new supplements or herbal remedies. Some supplements may interact with medications or have adverse effects, and their safety and efficacy are not always well-established.
  • Stress Management: Chronic stress can have a negative impact on blood sugar levels and overall health. Incorporate stress-reducing activities such as meditation, yoga, or relaxation techniques into your daily routine to help manage stress levels effectively.

    • Prevention

    Physical Activity:

  • Yoga: Yoga practice may help improve insulin sensitivity and reduce stress levels, which can benefit individuals with Type 2 diabetes. Certain yoga poses and breathing exercises are specifically designed to help manage diabetes.
  • Tai Chi: Tai Chi is a gentle form of exercise that combines movement and meditation. It has been shown to improve blood sugar control and reduce stress in people with Type 2 diabetes.

    • Weight Management:

  • Portion Control: Controlling portion sizes and eating smaller, more frequent meals throughout the day can help prevent spikes in blood sugar levels.
  • Intermittent Fasting: Some people find that intermittent fasting, which involves cycling between periods of eating and fasting, can help improve insulin sensitivity and promote weight loss. However, it's essential to consult with a healthcare professional before starting any fasting regimen, especially if you have diabetes.
