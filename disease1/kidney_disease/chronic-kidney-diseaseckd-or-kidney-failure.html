Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)

Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) is a long-term condition where the kidneys progressively lose their function. It can develop over months or years and often has no noticeable symptoms until significant damage has occurred.

Causes and Risk Factors

The main causes of CKD are:

  • High blood pressure (Hypertension).
  • Diabetes, particularly if poorly controlled.
  • Genetic conditions such as polycystic kidney disease.
  • Repeated urinary tract infections (UTIs).
  • Kidney infections or injury.

Risk factors for developing CKD include:

  • Family history of kidney disease.
  • Aging - CKD is more common as people age.
  • Obesity.
  • Smoking and excessive alcohol consumption.

Symptoms

Symptoms of CKD are often not apparent until the kidneys are severely damaged. They may include:

  • Fatigue or weakness.
  • Swelling in the legs, ankles, or feet.
  • Shortness of breath.
  • Frequent urination, especially at night.
  • Blood in the urine or foamy urine.
  • Persistent pain in the back or sides.

Treatment Options

Treatment for CKD focuses on slowing the progression of the disease and managing symptoms. Options include:

Medications:
  • Medications to control blood pressure, blood sugar, and cholesterol levels.
  • Diuretics to reduce fluid buildup in the body.
  • Phosphate binders for patients with kidney-related mineral imbalances.
Dialysis:
  • In advanced stages of CKD, dialysis may be needed to replace the function of the kidneys. There are two types of dialysis: hemodialysis and peritoneal dialysis.
Kidney Transplant:
  • A kidney transplant may be an option for people with end-stage kidney failure.

Prevention Tips

To reduce the risk of CKD or slow its progression, the following lifestyle changes may help:

  • Control blood sugar levels if diabetic.
  • Maintain a healthy blood pressure.
  • Eat a balanced diet low in sodium and saturated fats.
  • Exercise regularly and maintain a healthy weight.
  • Quit smoking and limit alcohol intake.
