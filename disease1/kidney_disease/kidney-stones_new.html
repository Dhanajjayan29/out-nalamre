Kidney Stones

Kidney stones are solid masses made of crystals that form in the kidneys. They can cause severe pain and may block the urinary tract. The stones vary in size, ranging from tiny grains to large masses.

Causes and Risk Factors

Kidney stones form when the urine becomes concentrated, allowing minerals to crystallize and stick together. Causes include:

  • Dehydration - Not drinking enough water can lead to concentrated urine, increasing the risk of kidney stones.
  • Diet - A diet high in salt, sugar, or protein may increase the risk of kidney stones.
  • Obesity - Obesity is a risk factor for kidney stones.

Risk factors include:

  • Family history of kidney stones.
  • Chronic medical conditions such as high blood pressure or diabetes.
  • Certain medications, including diuretics and antacids containing calcium.
  • High levels of calcium or oxalate in urine.

Symptoms

Common symptoms of kidney stones include:

  • Severe pain in the back, side, or abdomen.
  • Blood in the urine.
  • Frequent urination or pain during urination.
  • Cloudy or foul-smelling urine.

Treatment Options

The treatment for kidney stones depends on the size and type of stone. Options include:

Medical Treatment:
  • Small stones may pass on their own with increased fluid intake and pain relievers.
  • For larger stones, medications may help break the stones into smaller pieces.
Surgical Options:
  • Shock wave lithotripsy - A procedure that uses sound waves to break kidney stones into smaller pieces.
  • Ureteroscopy - A procedure where a thin tube is inserted to remove or break the stone.
  • Percutaneous nephrolithotomy - A surgical procedure to remove very large stones.

Prevention Tips

To help prevent kidney stones, consider the following tips:

  • Drink plenty of water to keep your urine diluted.
  • Avoid excessive salt and animal protein in your diet.
  • Eat more fruits and vegetables.
  • Limit foods high in oxalates, such as spinach and nuts, if you're prone to kidney stones.

    Kidney stones

    • Nalamaree Team
    • 25 April 2024

    Overview

    A kidney stone, also known as renal calculus, is a solid mass made up of tiny crystals that form in the kidneys from substances found in the urine. These substances can include calcium, oxalate, uric acid, and others. Kidney stones vary in size, from as small as a grain of sand to as large as a golf ball.

    Causes

    When the concentration of these substances in the urine becomes too high, or when there isn't enough fluid to dilute them, they can crystallize and stick together to form a stone. Kidney stones can cause severe pain as they pass through the urinary tract, including the ureters (the tubes that connect the kidneys to the bladder), the bladder, and the urethra (the tube through which urine passes out of the body).

    Kidney stones form when certain substances in the urine, such as calcium, oxalate, and uric acid, become highly concentrated and crystallize. There are several factors that can contribute to the formation of kidney stones:

  • Dehydration: When you don't drink enough fluids, your urine becomes more concentrated, increasing the likelihood of crystal formation.
  • Diet: Consuming foods high in oxalate, such as spinach, nuts, chocolate, and certain teas, can contribute to the formation of calcium oxalate stones. Similarly, a diet high in sodium (salt) can increase calcium levels in the urine, which may lead to the formation of calcium-containing stones.
  • Medical conditions: Certain medical conditions, such as hyperparathyroidism, gout, and urinary tract infections, can increase the risk of kidney stone formation.
  • Genetics: Some people may have a genetic predisposition to forming kidney stones.
  • Obesity: Being overweight or obese can increase the risk of kidney stones.
  • Medications: Certain medications, such as diuretics, antacids containing calcium, and certain antibiotics, can increase the risk of kidney stone formation.
  • Medical procedures: Certain medical procedures, such as gastric bypass surgery or other intestinal surgeries, can alter the digestive process, leading to changes in the urinary system that may increase the risk of kidney stones.

    • Symptoms

    The symptoms of kidney stones can vary depending on the size of the stone, its location within the urinary tract, and whether it is causing a blockage or irritation. Common symptoms of kidney stones include:

  • Severe pain: This is often the most prominent symptom and is typically felt in the back, side, abdomen, or groin. The pain can be intermittent or constant and may come in waves. It is often described as sharp, stabbing, or cramping.
  • Painful urination: Individuals with kidney stones may experience pain or discomfort when urinating. This can include a burning sensation or a sense of urgency.
  • Blood in the urine (hematuria): Kidney stones can cause blood to appear in the urine, giving it a pink, red, or brown color. The presence of blood in the urine may be visible to the naked eye or detected through laboratory tests.
  • Frequent urination: Some people with kidney stones may feel the need to urinate more frequently than usual, often in small amounts.
  • Nausea and vomiting: The pain associated with kidney stones can trigger nausea and vomiting in some individuals.
  • Urinary urgency and hesitancy: Kidney stones can cause changes in urinary habits, including a sense of urgency to urinate and difficulty starting urination.
  • Fever and chills: In cases where a kidney stone causes a urinary tract infection (UTI), individuals may experience fever, chills, and other signs of infection.

    • Treatment: Modern Medicine

  • Pain management: prescription for pain medications may be used to manage the severe pain associated with kidney stones.
  • Hydration: Drinking plenty of fluids helps to flush out the urinary system and may help small stones pass more easily.
  • Medications: Depending on the composition of the kidney stones, medications may be prescribed to help dissolve the stones or prevent them from forming. For example, alpha-blockers can help relax the muscles in the ureter, making it easier for stones to pass.
  • Extracorporeal shock wave lithotripsy (ESWL): This non-invasive procedure uses shock waves to break up kidney stones into smaller pieces that can be passed more easily through the urinary tract.
  • Ureteroscopy: A thin, flexible scope is inserted into the urinary tract to locate and remove the kidney stones. This procedure may be used for larger stones or those that are difficult to reach with other methods.
  • Percutaneous nephrolithotomy (PCNL): In this minimally invasive procedure, a small incision is made in the back, and a nephroscope is inserted to remove larger kidney stones directly from the kidney.
  • Laser lithotripsy: This technique uses laser energy to break up kidney stones into smaller fragments, which can then be passed naturally or removed with a scope.
  • Surgery: In rare cases where other treatments are ineffective or if there are complications such as a large stone causing kidney damage, open surgery may be necessary to remove the kidney stone.

    • Treatment: Traditional Medicine

  • Dietary changes: Avoiding foods high in oxalate, such as spinach, rhubarb, nuts, and chocolate, may help reduce the risk of calcium oxalate stones. Limiting intake of foods high in sodium and animal proteins can also be beneficial.
  • Lemon juice: Citric acid found in lemon juice may help prevent the formation of certain types of kidney stones. Drinking diluted lemon juice or adding lemon to water may help increase citrate levels in the urine, which can inhibit stone formation.
  • Apple cider vinegar: Some people believe that drinking diluted apple cider vinegar can help dissolve kidney stones and alleviate pain. However, there is limited scientific evidence to support this claim, and excessive consumption of vinegar can be harmful to tooth enamel and the digestive system.
  • Herbal remedies: Certain herbs, such as chanca piedra, dandelion root, and parsley, are believed to have diuretic properties and may help promote kidney health. However, more research is needed to confirm their effectiveness and safety for kidney stone treatment.
  • Baking soda: Drinking a mixture of water and baking soda may help raise the pH level of urine, making it less acidic and potentially reducing the risk of uric acid stone formation. However, this remedy should be used cautiously, as excessive intake of baking soda can lead to electrolyte imbalances and other health issues.
  • Magnesium supplements: Magnesium may help prevent the formation of calcium oxalate stones by binding to oxalate in the digestive tract, reducing its absorption into the bloodstream and excretion in the urine. 

    • Caution

  • Be mindful of supplements: Certain supplements, especially high doses of Vitamin C, can increase the risk of stones. Always discuss supplements with your doctor before taking them.
  • Pay attention to your body: If you experience symptoms like severe pain in your flank or abdomen, blood in your urine, nausea, or difficulty urinating, seek medical attention immediately.

    • Prevention

  • Monitor urine pH: If you have a history of certain types of kidney stones, such as uric acid stones, monitoring urine pH levels may be beneficial. Maintaining a slightly alkaline urine pH can help prevent uric acid stone formation. Your healthcare provider can provide guidance on monitoring and adjusting urine pH as needed.
  • Limit alcohol and caffeine: Excessive consumption of alcohol and caffeine can increase urinary calcium excretion, potentially contributing to stone formation. Limit intake of alcoholic and caffeinated beverages and opt for water or herbal teas instead.
  • Maintain a healthy weight: Obesity and overweight are risk factors for kidney stones. Adopting a healthy diet and engaging in regular physical activity can help achieve and maintain a healthy weight, reducing the risk of stone formation.
  • Exercise regularly: Regular physical activity can help prevent kidney stones by promoting overall health and reducing the risk of obesity and other metabolic conditions associated with stone formation.
  • Stay hydrated: Aim for at least 2-3 liters of water per day. This helps dilute urine and flush out minerals that could potentially form stones.
  • Monitor your diet: Limit sodium, animal protein, and oxalate-rich foods like spinach and nuts (depending on the type of stone you have). Consult a doctor or registered dietitian for personalized dietary advice.
