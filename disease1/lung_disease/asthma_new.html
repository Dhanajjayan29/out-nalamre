Overview

Asthma is a chronic respiratory condition that affects the airways in the lungs. In individuals with asthma, the airways become inflamed and swollen, making them more sensitive to certain triggers. When exposed to these triggers, the airways may constrict, making it difficult for air to pass through. This leads to symptoms such as wheezing, coughing, shortness of breath, and chest tightness.

Asthma is typically classified into two main types:

  • Allergic asthma: Triggered by exposure to allergens such as pollen, dust mites, pet dander, or mold.
  • Non-allergic asthma: Triggered by factors such as respiratory infections, exercise, cold air, smoke, pollution, or emotional stress.

Causes

  • Genetic predisposition: Asthma tends to run in families, suggesting that genetic factors play a role in its development. People with a family history of asthma or other allergic conditions are at higher risk of developing asthma themselves.
  • Allergens: Common allergens associated with asthma include pollen, dust mites, pet dander, mold, and cockroach droppings.
  • Respiratory infections: Viral respiratory infections, such as the common cold, influenza, or respiratory syncytial virus (RSV), can trigger asthma symptoms.
  • Environmental factors: Exposure to environmental pollutants like tobacco smoke, air pollution, and chemical irritants.
  • Exercise and physical activity: Exercise-induced asthma is triggered by physical exertion or exercise.
  • Weather changes: Cold air, humidity, and changes in temperature can trigger asthma symptoms.
  • Emotional factors: Stress, anxiety, and strong emotions can worsen asthma symptoms.
  • Other factors: Factors like certain medications and medical conditions (e.g., GERD) may contribute to asthma development or exacerbation.

Symptoms

  • Wheezing: A high-pitched whistling sound when breathing out.
  • Coughing: Persistent coughing that may worsen at night or early in the morning.
  • Shortness of breath: Difficulty breathing or feeling like you can't get enough air.
  • Chest tightness: A sensation of pressure or squeezing in the chest.
  • Difficulty sleeping: Asthma symptoms often worsen at night, making it hard to sleep.
  • Difficulty speaking: Severe asthma symptoms can impair speech.
  • Coughing or wheezing during exercise: Exercise-induced asthma is common in some individuals.

Treatment: Modern Medicine

  • Inhaled corticosteroids: Reduces inflammation in the airways (e.g., Fluticasone, Budesonide).
  • Long-acting beta-agonists (LABAs): Relax the muscles around the airways (e.g., Salmeterol, Formoterol).
  • Short-acting beta-agonists (SABAs): Provide rapid relief during an asthma attack (e.g., Albuterol, Levalbuterol).
  • Leukotriene modifiers: Help reduce inflammation (e.g., Montelukast, Zafirlukast).
  • Combination inhalers: Contain both corticosteroids and beta-agonists (e.g., Advair, Symbicort).
  • Biologic therapies: For severe asthma (e.g., Omalizumab, Mepolizumab).
  • Oral corticosteroids: Prescribed for short-term use during severe episodes (e.g., Prednisone).
  • Allergy shots (immunotherapy): Reduces sensitivity to allergens.

Treatment: Traditional Medicine

  • Breathing exercises: Techniques like pursed-lip breathing can help improve airflow.
  • Yoga and tai chi: Mind-body practices that improve lung function.
  • Herbal remedies: Herbs like ginger, turmeric, and eucalyptus may reduce inflammation and improve breathing.
  • Acupuncture: Some studies suggest acupuncture may help manage asthma symptoms.
  • Aromatherapy: Essential oils like eucalyptus or peppermint may help alleviate congestion and improve breathing.

    Asthma

    • Nalamaree Team
    • 16 April 2024

    Overview

    Asthma is a chronic respiratory condition that affects the airways in the lungs. In individuals with asthma, the airways become inflamed and swollen, making them more sensitive to certain triggers. When exposed to these triggers, the airways may constrict, making it difficult for air to pass through. This leads to symptoms such as wheezing, coughing, shortness of breath, and chest tightness.
    Asthma is typically classified into two main types:
  • Allergic asthma : Allergic asthma is triggered by exposure to allergens such as pollen, dust mites, pet dander, or mold.
  • Non-allergic asthma: Non-allergic asthma, on the other hand, can be triggered by factors such as respiratory infections, exercise, cold air, smoke, pollution, or emotional stress.

    • Causes

  • Genetic predisposition: Asthma tends to run in families, suggesting that genetic factors play a role in its development. People with a family history of asthma or other allergic conditions are at higher risk of developing asthma themselves.
  • Allergens: Allergens are substances that can trigger allergic reactions in susceptible individuals. Common allergens associated with asthma include pollen, dust mites, pet dander, mold, and cockroach droppings. Exposure to these allergens can lead to inflammation of the airways and asthma symptoms.
  • Respiratory infections: Viral respiratory infections, such as the common cold, influenza, or respiratory syncytial virus (RSV), can trigger asthma symptoms or exacerbate existing asthma. Infections can cause inflammation and irritation of the airways, making them more reactive to other triggers.
  • Environmental factors: Exposure to environmental pollutants, such as tobacco smoke, air pollution, chemical irritants, and occupational dusts or fumes, can increase the risk of developing asthma or exacerbate existing asthma symptoms. These pollutants can irritate the airways and trigger inflammation.
  • Exercise and physical activity: Exercise-induced asthma is a type of asthma triggered by physical exertion or exercise. It is more common in people with underlying asthma but can also occur in individuals without a history of asthma.
  • Weather changes: Cold air, humidity, changes in temperature, and weather extremes can trigger asthma symptoms in some individuals.
  • Emotional factors: Stress, anxiety, and strong emotions can sometimes trigger asthma symptoms or exacerbate existing asthma. While emotional factors alone are not typically the primary cause of asthma, they can contribute to symptom worsening in some individuals.
  • Other factors: Other factors that may contribute to the development or exacerbation of asthma include exposure to certain medications (such as aspirin or nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs), hormonal changes (such as those associated with pregnancy or menstrual cycles), and certain medical conditions (such as gastroesophageal reflux disease).

    • Symptoms

  • Wheezing: Wheezing is a high-pitched whistling sound that occurs when breathing out. It is caused by narrowed airways and is a classic symptom of asthma.
  • Coughing: Asthma-related coughing can be persistent and may worsen at night or early in the morning. Coughing may be the only symptom present in some individuals, especially in children.
  • Shortness of breath: Shortness of breath, or dyspnea, is a sensation of difficulty breathing or feeling like you can't get enough air. It may occur during physical activity or at rest.
  • Chest tightness: Asthma-related chest tightness is often described as a feeling of pressure or squeezing in the chest. It may feel like someone is sitting on your chest or like there is a tight band around it.
  • Difficulty sleeping: Asthma symptoms can worsen at night, leading to difficulty sleeping or frequent awakenings due to coughing, wheezing, or shortness of breath.
  • Difficulty speaking: Severe asthma symptoms can make it difficult to speak in full sentences or carry on a conversation.
  • Coughing or wheezing during exercise: Exercise-induced asthma is a common type of asthma triggered by physical exertion or exercise. Symptoms typically occur during or shortly after exercise and may include coughing, wheezing, chest tightness, or shortness of breath.

    • Treatment: Modern Medicine

  • Inhaled corticosteroids: These medications are the cornerstone of asthma treatment and work by reducing inflammation in the airways. They are usually taken daily to prevent asthma symptoms and exacerbations. Examples include fluticasone (Flovent), Budesonide (Pulmicort), and Beclomethasone (Qvar).
  • Long-acting beta-agonists (LABAs): LABAs are bronchodilators that help to relax the muscles around the airways, making it easier to breathe. They are often used in combination with inhaled corticosteroids for better asthma control. Examples include Salmeterol (Serevent) and Formoterol (Foradil).
  • Short-acting beta-agonists (SABAs): SABAs are quick-relief medications that provide rapid relief of asthma symptoms during an asthma attack or exacerbation. They work by quickly relaxing the muscles around the airways. Examples include Albuterol (ProAir, Ventolin) and Levalbuterol (Xopenex).
  • Leukotriene modifiers: These medications help to reduce inflammation in the airways and prevent asthma symptoms by blocking the action of leukotrienes, which are chemicals involved in the inflammatory response. Examples include Montelukast (Singulair) and Zafirlukast (Accolate).
  • Combination inhalers: These inhalers contain both an inhaled corticosteroid and a long-acting beta-agonist in a single device. They are used for daily maintenance treatment of asthma symptoms and prevention of asthma attacks. Examples include Fluticasone/Salmeterol (Advair) and Budesonide/Formoterol (Symbicort).
  • Biologic therapies: These medications are used for severe asthma that does not respond well to other treatments. They work by targeting specific immune system proteins involved in the inflammatory response. Examples include Omalizumab (Xolair), Mepolizumab (Nucala), and Dupilumab (Dupixent).
  • Oral corticosteroids: In severe cases or during asthma exacerbations, oral corticosteroids may be prescribed for short-term use to help reduce inflammation and control symptoms. Examples include Prednisone and Prednisolone.
  • Allergy shots (immunotherapy): For individuals with allergic asthma triggered by specific allergens, allergy shots may be recommended to help reduce sensitivity to those allergens over time.

    • Treatment: Traditional Medicine

  • Breathing exercises: Techniques such as pursed-lip breathing and diaphragmatic breathing may help improve breathing efficiency and reduce asthma symptoms. These exercises focus on slow, deep breathing to promote relaxation and open the airways.
  • Yoga and tai chi: These mind-body practices incorporate gentle movements, stretching, and controlled breathing exercises, which can help reduce stress and improve lung function in some people with asthma.
  • Herbal remedies: Some herbs and botanicals have been used traditionally to help manage asthma symptoms. Examples include ginger, turmeric, garlic, and licorice root. However, it's essential to consult with a healthcare provider before using herbal remedies, as they may interact with medications or exacerbate asthma symptoms in some individuals.
  • Steam inhalation: Inhaling steam from a bowl of hot water or a steam shower may help loosen mucus and relieve congestion in the airways, making it easier to breathe. Adding essential oils such as eucalyptus or peppermint oil to the steam may provide additional relief for some people.
  • Honey: Some people with asthma find that consuming honey, especially locally sourced honey, may help alleviate asthma symptoms. However, scientific evidence supporting the use of honey for asthma is limited, and more research is needed to determine its effectiveness.
  • Breathing techniques: Some traditional breathing techniques, such as the Buteyko method, involve shallow breathing exercises aimed at reducing hyperventilation and improving respiratory function. These techniques may be beneficial for some individuals with asthma, but they should be learned under the guidance of a qualified instructor.
  • Acupuncture: Acupuncture, a traditional Chinese medicine practice involving the insertion of thin needles into specific points on the body, has been studied as a potential treatment for asthma. While some research suggests that acupuncture may help improve asthma symptoms and reduce the need for medication in some individuals, more studies are needed to confirm its effectiveness.
  • Dietary modifications: Some people with asthma find that certain dietary changes, such as reducing intake of processed foods, sugar, and dairy products, and increasing consumption of fruits, vegetables, and omega-3 fatty acids, may help improve asthma control. 

    • Caution

  • Consult with a healthcare provider: Before trying any new traditional methods or home remedies for asthma, it's essential to consult with a healthcare provider, preferably one who is knowledgeable about asthma management. They can provide personalized advice and guidance based on your individual health status and medical history.
  • Do not replace prescribed medications: Traditional methods and home remedies should not be used as a substitute for prescribed asthma medications. Asthma is a serious medical condition that requires proper medical treatment to control symptoms and prevent asthma attacks. It's important to continue following the prescribed asthma management plan and to take prescribed medications as directed by your healthcare provider.
  • Be aware of potential interactions: Traditional methods and home remedies may interact with prescribed medications or other treatments you are using. It's important to discuss any new treatments with your healthcare provider to ensure they are safe and appropriate for you, especially if you are already taking medications for asthma or other medical conditions.
  • Avoid known triggers: Some traditional methods and home remedies may involve exposure to allergens or other asthma triggers. It's important to be aware of your asthma triggers and to avoid any treatments that may worsen your asthma symptoms or trigger an asthma attack.
  • Be cautious with herbal remedies: Herbal remedies can have pharmacological effects and may interact with medications or exacerbate asthma symptoms in some individuals. It's essential to consult with a healthcare provider before using herbal remedies, especially if you are taking prescribed medications or have other medical conditions.

    • Prevention

  • Identify and avoid triggers: Learn about your asthma triggers and take steps to avoid or minimize exposure to them. Common triggers include allergens (such as pollen, dust mites, pet dander, and mold), respiratory infections, air pollution, tobacco smoke, cold air, exercise, stress, and certain medications.
  • Maintain a healthy environment: Keep your home clean and free of dust, mold, and other allergens. Use allergen-proof mattress and pillow covers, wash bedding regularly in hot water, vacuum carpets and upholstery frequently, and use air purifiers or dehumidifiers as needed. Avoid smoking indoors and minimize exposure to environmental pollutants.
  • Manage allergies: If you have allergies that trigger your asthma symptoms, work with a healthcare provider to develop an allergy management plan. This may include allergen avoidance strategies, medications (such as antihistamines or nasal corticosteroids), and allergy shots (immunotherapy) to reduce sensitivity to allergens over time.
  • Stay physically active: Regular physical activity can help improve lung function and overall health, which may reduce the severity and frequency of asthma symptoms. Choose activities that you enjoy and can safely perform, and consider using a rescue inhaler before exercise if exercise-induced asthma is a concern.
  • Follow a healthy lifestyle: Maintain a healthy diet rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and lean proteins, and stay hydrated by drinking plenty of water. Aim to maintain a healthy weight, as obesity can worsen asthma symptoms. Get enough sleep each night and manage stress through relaxation techniques such as deep breathing, meditation, or yoga.
  • Practice good asthma management: Work closely with a healthcare provider to develop and follow an asthma management plan tailored to your individual needs. Take prescribed medications as directed, including long-term control medications to prevent asthma symptoms and rescue inhalers to provide quick relief during asthma attacks. Monitor your asthma symptoms regularly and seek medical attention if symptoms worsen or if you have difficulty controlling your asthma.
