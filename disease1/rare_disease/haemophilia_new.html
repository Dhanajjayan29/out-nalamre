    Haemophilia

    • Nalamaree Team
    • 16 April 2024

    Overview

    Hemophilia is a genetic disorder characterized by a deficiency in certain blood clotting factors, specifically factors VIII or IX. This deficiency impairs the blood's ability to clot normally, leading to prolonged bleeding even from minor injuries. Hemophilia is typically inherited, meaning it's passed down through families, most often affecting males while females can be carriers of the gene.

    There are different types of hemophilia:

  • Hemophilia A: This is the most common type, caused by a deficiency in clotting factor VIII.
  • Hemophilia B: Also known as Christmas disease, it is caused by a deficiency in clotting factor IX.
  • Haemophilia C: Results from low levels of factor XI.
  • Other types include Von Willebrand disease (low von Willebrand factor) and parahaemophilia (low factor V).

    • Causes

    Hemophilia is caused by a genetic mutation that affects the production of certain blood clotting factors. It is an inherited condition, typically passed down from parent to child through one or more abnormal genes on the X chromosome. In most cases, hemophilia primarily affects males, as they have only one X chromosome, while females have two, providing a backup copy of the gene in case one is faulty. However, it's important to note that females can also be carriers of the condition.

    The severity of hemophilia can vary depending on the level of clotting factor in the blood. Spontaneous mutations can also cause hemophilia in people with no family history of the disorder, but this is very rare. It's essential for individuals with a family history of hemophilia to seek genetic counseling and testing, especially if they're planning to have children, as this can provide important information about the risk of passing the condition to their offspring. 

    Symptoms

  • Prolonged bleeding: Individuals with hemophilia may experience prolonged bleeding following injuries, surgeries, or dental procedures. The bleeding may take longer than usual to stop and may be difficult to control.

  • Easy bruising: Hemophilia can cause easy bruising, even from minor bumps or injuries. Bruises may be larger than normal and take longer to heal.

  • Joint pain and swelling: Bleeding into joints, such as the knees, ankles, and elbows, is a common symptom of hemophilia. This can lead to pain, swelling, warmth, and limited range of motion in the affected joints.

  • Muscle bleeding: Bleeding into muscles can cause pain, swelling, and tightness in the affected area. This may occur spontaneously or following minor injuries.

  • Excessive bleeding from cuts or wounds: Individuals with hemophilia may experience excessive bleeding from cuts, wounds, or minor injuries. This bleeding may be difficult to stop with standard first aid measures.

  • Blood in urine or stool: Hemophilia can sometimes cause bleeding in the urinary or gastrointestinal tract, leading to blood in the urine or stool.

  • Nosebleeds: Recurrent nosebleeds may occur in individuals with hemophilia, particularly if the condition is severe.

    • Treatment: Modern Medicine

  • Replacement Therapy: The cornerstone of modern hemophilia treatment involves replacing the missing or deficient clotting factor. This is typically done through intravenous infusion of clotting factor concentrates derived from human plasma or through recombinant technology.
  • Prophylactic Treatment: Many patients with severe hemophilia receive regular prophylactic infusions of clotting factor concentrates to prevent bleeding episodes and joint damage.
  • On-Demand Treatment: Patients may also receive clotting factor infusions on-demand to manage bleeding episodes.
  • Gene Therapy: Emerging treatments like gene therapy aim to address the underlying genetic cause of hemophilia by introducing functional genes into patients' cells to produce clotting factors.
  • Hemostatic Agents: In addition to clotting factor replacement, medications such as desmopressin or antifibrinolytics may be used to help control bleeding in some cases.
  • Physical Therapy: Physical therapy is often prescribed to maintain joint health and mobility, particularly in patients with recurrent joint bleeding.

    • Treatment: Traditional Medicine

  • Herbal remedies: Some herbs, such as arnica and turmeric, have been shown to have anti-inflammatory and pain-relieving properties, which may be helpful for people with hemophilia.
  • Yoga and tai chi: These gentle exercises can help to improve flexibility and range of motion, which may be beneficial for people with hemophilia.
  • Acupuncture and acupressure: These traditional Chinese medicine techniques may help to reduce pain and inflammation.

    • Caution

  • Avoid activities that could cause injury. This includes contact sports, high-impact activities, and activities that involve sharp objects
  • Talk to your doctor about medications. Some medications, such as aspirin and ibuprofen, can thin the blood and increase the risk of bleeding.
  • Be aware of the signs and symptoms of bleeding.

    • These include:

  •  Easy bruising
  • Excessive bleeding from cuts or scrapes
  • Joint pain and swelling
  • Blood in the stool or urine.

    • Prevention

  •       Get regular exercise:This can help to strengthen muscles and joints and reduce the risk of injury.
  •       Maintain a healthy weight: Being overweight or obese can increase the risk of bleeding.
  •      Get vaccinated: This can help to protect you from infections that could lead to bleeding.  
  •       ﻿ Join a support group:Talking to other people with hemophilia can be helpful in coping with the challenges of the condition.
