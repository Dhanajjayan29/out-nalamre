Cystic Fibrosis

Overview

Cystic fibrosis (CF) is a genetic disorder that primarily affects the lungs and digestive system. It is caused by mutations in the Cystic Fibrosis Transmembrane Conductance Regulator (CFTR) gene, which regulates chloride and water movement across cell membranes. Mutations lead to defective or absent CFTR protein, resulting in thick, sticky mucus buildup.

Causes

  • Inheritance: CF is an autosomal recessive disorder, requiring two mutated CFTR genes (one from each parent) for the condition to manifest.
  • CFTR Gene Mutations: Over 1,700 mutations are known, with F508del being the most common. These mutations cause a defective CFTR protein, impacting various organs.
  • Effects on Organs:
    • Lungs: Thick mucus leads to lung infections and respiratory failure.
    • Digestive System: Mucus blocks pancreatic ducts, causing nutrient malabsorption.
    • Other Organs: Affects liver, reproductive system, and sweat glands.

Symptoms

  • Persistent coughing with phlegm
  • Frequent lung infections (bronchitis or pneumonia)
  • Wheezing or shortness of breath
  • Poor growth despite a good appetite
  • Foul-smelling, greasy stools
  • Intestinal blockage (meconium ileus in newborns)
  • Nasal polyps
  • Saltier-than-normal skin
  • Difficulty with bowel movements

Treatment: Modern Medicine

  • CFTR Modulator Therapies: Drugs like ivacaftor, Orkambi, and Trikafta target the CFTR protein, improving lung function and quality of life.
  • Airway Clearance Techniques: Chest physiotherapy, HFCWO, and PEP therapy help clear mucus from airways.
  • Lung Transplantation: Considered for severe lung disease, improving survival and quality of life.
  • Multidisciplinary Care: A team approach involving pulmonologists, dietitians, and social workers.
  • Antibiotics: Inhaled antibiotics manage recurrent lung infections and reduce exacerbations.

Treatment: Traditional Medicine

  • Symptomatic Management: Bronchodilators and mucolytics are used to manage symptoms.
  • Nutritional Support: High-calorie diets and enzyme supplements improve nutrient absorption.
  • Aggressive Infection Management: Antibiotic therapy for recurrent infections, with careful selection to avoid resistance.

Caution

  • Infection Control: Practice good hygiene to prevent respiratory infections.
  • Lung Health: Avoid tobacco smoke and adhere to airway clearance techniques.
  • Mental Health Support: Counseling and support groups may help cope with the chronic illness burden.
  • Pregnancy Planning: Close monitoring is needed for pregnant CF patients to optimize outcomes.
  • Travel Precautions: Plan ahead to minimize infection risks and ensure medication availability.

Prevention

  • Genetic Counseling and Testing: Helps assess the risk of having a child with CF.
  • Carrier Testing: Identifies carriers of CFTR gene mutations to inform reproductive decisions.
  • Prenatal Testing: Tests like CVS and amniocentesis can diagnose CF in fetuses.
  • Preimplantation Genetic Diagnosis (PGD): Allows selection of embryos without CF mutations during IVF.
  • Newborn Screening: Early diagnosis improves quality of life and life expectancy.
