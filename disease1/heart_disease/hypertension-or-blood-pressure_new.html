    Hypertension or Blood Pressure

    • Nalamaree Team
    • 24 April 2024

    Overview

    Hypertension, commonly known as high blood pressure, is a medical condition characterized by elevated blood pressure in the arteries. Blood pressure is the force of blood pushing against the walls of the arteries as the heart pumps blood throughout the body.

    Blood pressure is measured in millimeters of mercury (mmHg) and typically recorded as two numbers: systolic pressure (the pressure when the heart beats) over diastolic pressure (the pressure when the heart is at rest between beats). Normal blood pressure is typically considered to be around 120/80 mmHg.

    Causes

  • Genetics: Family history plays a significant role in determining an individual's risk of developing hypertension. If your parents or close relatives have high blood pressure, you may be at a higher risk.
  • Unhealthy Lifestyle Choices: Poor diet, lack of physical activity, excessive alcohol consumption, and smoking are all lifestyle factors that can contribute to the development of hypertension. Diets high in sodium (salt) and low in potassium can increase blood pressure.
  • Obesity: Being overweight or obese puts extra strain on the heart and increases the risk of developing hypertension.
  • Stress: Chronic stress can lead to hormonal changes in the body that may increase blood pressure over time.
  • Age: Blood pressure tends to increase with age due to changes in the elasticity of blood vessels and the function of the heart.
  • Certain Medical Conditions: Certain medical conditions can increase the risk of hypertension, including diabetes, kidney disease, thyroid disorders, and sleep apnea.
  • Medications: Some medications, such as certain types of birth control pills, cold remedies, decongestants, and nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), can raise blood pressure.
  • Other Factors: Other factors that may contribute to hypertension include high levels of certain hormones (such as cortisol and adrenaline), chronic kidney disease, and certain congenital defects in blood vessels.

  • Symptoms

    Hypertension is often referred to as a "silent killer" because it typically doesn't cause noticeable symptoms until it has reached an advanced stage or triggered a medical emergency like a heart attack or stroke. However, some people may experience symptoms related to extremely high blood pressure. These symptoms can include:
  • Severe Headaches: Particularly when associated with high blood pressure, these headaches tend to occur in the morning and may be accompanied by dizziness or blurred vision.
  • Vision Problems: Blurred vision or difficulty seeing can occur when hypertension affects blood flow to the eyes.
  • Chest Pain: Also known as angina, chest pain or tightness may occur when the heart is not receiving enough oxygen due to high blood pressure.
  • Shortness of Breath: Difficulty breathing or shortness of breath may occur, especially with physical exertion, due to the heart having to work harder to pump blood.
  • Irregular Heartbeat (Arrhythmia): High blood pressure can disrupt the normal rhythm of the heart, leading to palpitations or irregular heartbeat.
  • Nosebleeds: While not a common symptom, some people with very high blood pressure may experience frequent nosebleeds.
  • Fatigue: Feeling unusually tired or fatigued can be a symptom of hypertension, particularly if it's causing complications such as heart failure.

    • Treatment: Modern Medicine

  • Thiazide Diuretics: Hydrochlorothiazide (HCTZ), chlorthalidone, and indapamide. They work by reducing the amount of water and sodium in the body, thus lowering blood pressure.
  • Angiotensin-Converting Enzyme (ACE) Inhibitors: Lisinopril, Enalapril, and Ramipril. ACE inhibitors block the production of Angiotensin II, a hormone that narrows blood vessels, leading to vasodilation and decreased blood pressure.
  • Angiotensin II Receptor Blockers (ARBs): Losartan, Valsartan, and Irbesartan. ARBs block the action of Angiotensin II on blood vessels, causing relaxation and lowering blood pressure.
  • Dihydropyridine Calcium Channel Blockers: Amlodipine, Nifedipine, and Felodipine. They relax blood vessels by blocking calcium from entering cells, leading to reduced blood pressure.
  • Non-Dihydropyridine Calcium Channel Blockers:  Diltiazem and Verapamil. They also relax blood vessels and can slow the heart rate.
  • Beta-Blockers: Metoprolol, Atenolol, and Carvedilol. Beta-blockers reduce heart rate and the force of heart contractions, thereby lowering blood pressure.\
  • Alpha-Blockers: Doxazosin and prazosin. Alpha-blockers relax certain muscles and arteries, leading to reduced blood pressure.
  • Direct Renin Inhibitors: Aliskiren. These medications directly inhibit the enzyme renin, which plays a key role in blood pressure regulation.
  • Central Agonists: Clonidine and Methyldopa. Central agonists act on the brain to reduce nerve signals that increase heart rate and narrow blood vessels, leading to lower blood pressure.

    • Treatment: Traditional Medicine

  • Dietary Changes: Increase Potassium Intake: Potassium-rich foods such as bananas, sweet potatoes, spinach, and avocados may help lower blood pressure by counteracting the effects of sodium.
  • Garlic: Some studies suggest that garlic may have blood pressure-lowering effects. Incorporating fresh garlic into meals or taking garlic supplements may be beneficial.
  • Hibiscus Tea: Drinking hibiscus tea, which contains compounds that may help lower blood pressure, has been shown in some studies to have mild anti hypertensive effects.
  • Omega-3 Fatty Acids: Consuming foods rich in omega-3 fatty acids, such as fatty fish (e.g., salmon, mackerel, sardines), flax seeds, and walnuts, may help lower blood pressure and reduce the risk of heart disease
  • Hawthorn: Hawthorn is a traditional remedy for heart health and may help lower blood pressure by relaxing blood vessels and improving blood flow.
  • Cinnamon: Some studies suggest that cinnamon may have blood pressure-lowering effects. Adding cinnamon to foods or beverages may be beneficial.
  • Ginger: Ginger has been studied for its potential anti hypertensive effects, possibly due to its ability to relax blood vessels.
  • Turmeric: Turmeric contains curcumin, which has antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties and may help lower blood pressure.﻿

    • Caution

  • Monitor your blood pressure at home: This can help you track your progress and identify any trends.
  • Avoid smoking: Smoking is a major risk factor for heart disease and stroke. If you smoke, quitting is one of the best things you can do for your health.
  • Get enough sleep: Aim for 7-8 hours of sleep each night.
  • Limit caffeine: Caffeine can raise your blood pressure. If you are sensitive to caffeine, you may want to limit your intake or avoid it altogether.
  • Limit Sodium Intake: Reducing the amount of salt (sodium) in the diet can help lower blood pressure. Avoiding processed and packaged foods, which often contain high levels of sodium, and using herbs and spices to flavor foods instead of salt can be helpful.

    • Prevention

    • Healthy diet: Eating a healthy diet is one of the most important things you can do to manage your blood pressure. Limit your intake of salt, saturated and trans fats, and added sugars. Focus on eating plenty of fruits, vegetables, and whole grains.
    • Regular exercise: Aim for at least 30 minutes of moderate-intensity exercise most days of the week. Exercise helps to lower blood pressure and improve overall cardiovascular health.
    • Maintain a healthy weight: Being overweight or obese can increase your risk of high blood pressure. Losing weight, even if it's just a small amount, can help to lower your blood pressure.
    • Manage stress: Stress can also contribute to high blood pressure. Find healthy ways to manage stress, such as yoga, meditation, or spending time in nature.
    • Medication: If lifestyle changes alone are not enough to control your blood pressure, your doctor may prescribe medication. It is important to take your medication as prescribed, even if you feel well.
    • Regular doctor visits: It is important to see your doctor regularly to monitor your blood pressure and make sure your treatment plan is working.
