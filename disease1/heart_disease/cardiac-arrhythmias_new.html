    Cardiac arrhythmias

    • Nalamaree Team
    • 24 April 2024

    Overview

    Cardiac arrhythmias refer to abnormalities in the heart's rhythm or rate of beating. Normally, the heart beats in a regular, coordinated rhythm, which is essential for proper blood circulation and delivery of oxygen to the body's tissues. However, in cases of arrhythmia, the heart may beat too fast (tachycardia), too slow (bradycardia), or irregularly.

    Causes

    Cardiac arrhythmias can be caused by various factors, including:

  • Heart disease: Conditions such as coronary artery disease, heart failure, heart valve disorders, congenital heart defects, or cardiomyopathy can disrupt the heart's normal electrical activity and lead to arrhythmias.
  • Electrolyte imbalances: Abnormal levels of electrolytes such as potassium, magnesium, or calcium in the body can affect the heart's electrical signals and trigger arrhythmias.
  • Structural abnormalities: Anomalies in the heart's structure, either congenital (present at birth) or acquired through conditions like myocardial infarction (heart attack), scar tissue formation, or cardiomyopathy, can predispose individuals to arrhythmias.
  • Age: The risk of arrhythmias increases with age, partly due to changes in the heart's electrical system and the presence of underlying medical conditions.
  • Genetics: Certain inherited conditions, such as long QT syndrome, Brugada syndrome, or familial atrial fibrillation, can predispose individuals to arrhythmias.
  • Medications and substances: Certain medications, particularly those that affect the heart's electrical conduction system, can increase the risk of arrhythmias. Additionally, substances such as alcohol, caffeine, nicotine, and illicit drugs can trigger arrhythmias in susceptible individuals.
  • Stress and emotional factors: Stress, anxiety, and intense emotional responses can stimulate the release of hormones that affect heart rate and rhythm, potentially leading to arrhythmias.
  • Other medical conditions: Various medical conditions, including thyroid disorders, diabetes, chronic kidney disease, and lung disease, can contribute to the development of arrhythmias.
  • Sleep apnea: Individuals with sleep-disordered breathing, such as obstructive sleep apnea, have an increased risk of developing arrhythmias, including atrial fibrillation.

    • Symptoms

    Common symptoms of cardiac arrhythmias include:

  • Palpitations: Feeling a fluttering, racing, pounding, or irregular heartbeat sensation in the chest is a common symptom of arrhythmias. Palpitations may be intermittent or persistent.
  • Dizziness or lightheadedness: Arrhythmias can disrupt blood flow to the brain, leading to feelings of dizziness, lightheadedness, or a sensation of nearly fainting (presyncope).
  • Fainting (syncope): In some cases, particularly with certain arrhythmias like ventricular tachycardia or bradyarrhythmias, the heart's abnormal rhythm can cause a sudden drop in blood pressure, leading to fainting episodes.
  • Chest pain or discomfort: Some arrhythmias, especially those associated with reduced blood flow to the heart muscle (ischemia), can cause chest pain or discomfort similar to angina.
  • Shortness of breath: Arrhythmias that impair the heart's ability to pump effectively or cause fluid buildup in the lungs can result in shortness of breath, especially with exertion or lying flat.
  • Fatigue: Feeling unusually tired or fatigued, even with minimal exertion, is a common symptom of arrhythmias, particularly when the heart's pumping efficiency is compromised.
  • Anxiety or feelings of impending doom: Some individuals may experience heightened anxiety or a sense of impending doom during arrhythmia episodes.
  • Sweating: Profuse sweating, especially when not related to exertion or ambient temperature, can occur during arrhythmia episodes.
  • Weakness or difficulty exercising: Arrhythmias can impair exercise tolerance and cause weakness or difficulty performing physical activities.
  • Other symptoms: Depending on the underlying cause and type of arrhythmia, individuals may experience additional symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, abdominal discomfort, neck discomfort, or symptoms related to associated conditions (e.g., heart failure, thyroid disorders).

    • Treatment: Modern Medicine

  • Antiarrhythmic medications: These drugs are used to control or prevent abnormal heart rhythms. They work by affecting the electrical signals in the heart.
  • Beta-blockers: Such as Metoprolol, Atenolol, or Propranolol, which slow the heart rate and reduce the heart's workload, particularly effective for supra ventricular arrhythmias.
  • Calcium channel blockers: Such as verapamil or diltiazem, which can slow the heart rate and reduce the strength of contractions, useful for certain types of supra ventricular arrhythmias.
  • Sodium channel blockers: Including medications like Flecainide, Propafenone, or Lidocaine, which help stabilize the heart's electrical activity and are used for various types of arrhythmias, including ventricular arrhythmias.
  • Potassium channel blockers: Such as Amiodarone, Sotalol, or Dofetilide, which are effective in treating a wide range of arrhythmias, including atrial fibrillation, ventricular tachycardia, and certain supra ventricular arrhythmias.
  • Blood-thinning medications (anticoagulants): For individuals with atrial fibrillation or other arrhythmias at increased risk of blood clots and stroke, anticoagulants such as warfarin, Dabigatran, Rivaroxaban, Apixaban, or Edoxaban may be prescribed to reduce the risk of stroke.
  • Medications to control underlying conditions: Treatment of underlying conditions such as heart failure, hypertension, thyroid disorders, or electrolyte imbalances may help reduce the risk of arrhythmias or alleviate symptoms.
  • Rate-controlling medications: In cases where the arrhythmia cannot be corrected, medications may be used to control the heart rate and improve symptoms. These medications are often used for atrial fibrillation or atrial flutter.
  • Electrolyte supplements: In cases where electrolyte imbalances contribute to arrhythmias, supplements such as potassium or magnesium may be prescribed to restore normal levels.

    • Treatment: Traditional Medicine

    Herbal Remedies:

  • Arjuna (Terminalia arjuna): This herb is commonly used in Ayurveda for heart health. It is believed to strengthen the heart muscle and regulate heart rhythm.
  • Hawthorn (Crataegus spp.): Hawthorn is used in traditional medicine to support cardiovascular health, including regulating heartbeat.
  • Garlic (Allium sativum): Garlic is known for its cardiovascular benefits, including its potential to regulate blood pressure and improve circulation, which may indirectly benefit heart rhythm.

    • Ayurvedic Formulations:

  • Dashmoolarishta: This Ayurvedic herbal tonic contains a blend of ten herbs and is traditionally used to support heart health and balance Vata dosha, which is associated with the nervous system and cardiovascular function.
  • Arjuna Ksheer Pak: A formulation prepared with Arjuna bark, milk, and other ingredients, it's used in Ayurveda to support heart function and maintain normal heart rhythm.

    • Caution

  • Avoidance of triggers: Identifying and avoiding triggers that can provoke arrhythmias, such as caffeine, alcohol, nicotine, certain medications, and illicit drugs, can help reduce the frequency and severity of episodes.
  • Limiting alcohol and caffeine: Alcohol and caffeine consumption can increase the risk of arrhythmias in susceptible individuals. Limiting or avoiding these substances may help reduce arrhythmia symptoms.
  • Quit smoking: Smoking is a risk factor for cardiovascular disease and can worsen arrhythmias. Quitting smoking can improve heart health and reduce the frequency and severity of arrhythmias.

    • Prevention

  • Dietary modifications: Adopting a heart-healthy diet can help manage cardiac arrhythmias by controlling blood pressure, cholesterol levels, and body weight. A diet rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, lean proteins, and healthy fats, while low in sodium, saturated fats, and added sugars, is recommended.
  • Regular exercise: Engaging in regular physical activity can improve cardiovascular health, reduce stress, and help maintain a healthy weight. Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic exercise or 75 minutes of vigorous-intensity exercise per week, as recommended by guidelines.
  • Stress management: Stress and anxiety can trigger or exacerbate arrhythmias in some individuals. Techniques such as deep breathing exercises, meditation, yoga, progressive muscle relaxation, or mindfulness practices can help reduce stress and promote relaxation.
  • Maintaining electrolyte balance: Ensuring adequate intake of electrolytes such as potassium, magnesium, and calcium through diet or supplementation may help stabilize the heart's electrical activity and prevent arrhythmias associated with electrolyte imbalances.
  • Maintaining a healthy weight: Obesity and excess body weight can increase the risk of developing arrhythmias and exacerbate existing ones. Achieving and maintaining a healthy weight through diet and exercise can help reduce this risk.
