    Diarrhea

    • Nalamaree Team
    • 16 April 2024

    Overview

    Diarrhea is a common condition characterized by loose, watery stools that occur more frequently than usual. It is often a symptom of an underlying issue, such as an infection, gastrointestinal disorder, or food intolerance. Diarrhea can be acute, lasting for a short period, or chronic, persisting for weeks to months. There are many different causes of diarrhoea. A bowel infection (Gastoenteritis) is a common cause in both adults and children. 

    Causes

  • Infections: Bacterial, viral, or parasitic infections are common causes of acute diarrhea. Contaminated food or water, poor hygiene, and contact with infected individuals can lead to these infections.
  • Gastrointestinal Disorders: Conditions such as irritable bowel syndrome (IBS), inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), and celiac disease can result in chronic diarrhea.
  • Food Intolerance: Some people may experience diarrhea due to an inability to digest certain foods or ingredients, such as lactose intolerance.
  • Medications: Certain medications, including antibiotics, can disrupt the normal balance of gut bacteria and lead to diarrhea.
  • Stress and Anxiety: Emotional stress and anxiety can affect the functioning of the digestive system, potentially leading to diarrhea.

    • Symptoms

  • Frequent, loose, and watery stools
  • Abdominal cramps or pain
  • Fever
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Dehydration (especially in severe cases)

    • Treatment: Modern Medicine

  • .Probiotics: Probiotics, which are beneficial bacteria, may help restore the balance of gut flora. They can be obtained through supplements or fermented foods like yogurt.
  • Fluid Replacement: Diarrhea can lead to dehydration due to the loss of fluids and electrolytes. Oral rehydration solutions (ORS), available over the counter, are recommended to replenish lost fluids and electrolytes. These solutions contain a precise balance of salts and sugars to help the body absorb water more efficiently. Severe cases of dehydration may require intravenous (IV) fluid replacement administered in a medical setting.
  • Antidiarrheal Medications: Over-the-counter antidiarrheal medications, such as Loperamide (Imodium) and Bismuth subsalicylate (Pepto-Bismol), can help reduce the frequency of bowel movements and relieve symptoms like abdominal cramping and urgency. These medications work by slowing down the movement of the intestines.
  •  Anti-Motility Agents: Medications like Diphenoxylate with Atropine (Lomotil) and Racecadotril (Hidrasec) are sometimes prescribed to reduce intestinal motility and fluid secretion in cases of acute diarrhea. These drugs can help alleviate symptoms but should be used cautiously and under medical supervision, as they may cause side effects and should not be used in certain conditions, such as diarrhea caused by bacterial infections or toxin exposure.

    • Treatment: Traditional Medicine

  • Hydration: The primary concern with diarrhea is dehydration due to the loss of fluids. It is essential to drink plenty of fluids, including water, oral rehydration solutions, and clear broths.
  • Dietary Changes: Gradually reintroduce easily digestible foods, such as bananas, rice, applesauce, and toast (BRAT diet). Avoid spicy, fatty, and dairy-rich foods until symptoms improve.

    • Herbal Teas and Infusions:

  • Chamomile tea: Known for its calming properties, chamomile tea can soothe the digestive system.
  • Ginger tea: Ginger has anti-inflammatory properties and may alleviate nausea and cramps.
  • Peppermint tea: Peppermint can ease stomach discomfort.
    • Herbal Remedies:
  • Psyllium husk: A natural source of soluble fiber that can bulk up stools.
  • Blackberry root bark: Traditionally used for diarrhea relief.

    • Caution

    • Avoid Certain Foods and Beverages: Some foods and beverages can exacerbate diarrhea or irritate the digestive system further.
    • Avoid spicy, fatty, and greasy foods, as well as caffeine and alcohol. 

    Prevention

    Hand Hygiene:

  • Wash your hands thoroughly with soap and water, especially after using the restroom and before handling food.
  • Use alcohol-based hand sanitizers when soap and water are not available.

    • Safe Water and Food:

  • Consume clean, safe drinking water. Boil or treat water if necessary.
  • Be cautious with street food and raw or undercooked foods. Ensure proper cooking and hygiene.

    • Food Handling and Storage:

  • Store perishable foods properly (refrigerate when needed).
  • Avoid cross-contamination by separating raw meats from other foods.
  • Wash fruits and vegetables thoroughly.

    • Vaccinations and Travel Precautions:

  • If traveling to areas with higher risk of diarrhea, consider vaccinations (e.g., cholera, rotavirus).
  • Follow travel advisories and take necessary precautions.
