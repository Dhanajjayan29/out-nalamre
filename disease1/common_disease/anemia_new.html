    Anemia

    • Nalamaree Team
    • 16 April 2024

    Overview

    Anemia is a medical condition characterized by a decrease in the number of red blood cells (RBCs) or a lower than normal amount of hemoglobin in the blood. Hemoglobin is a protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen from the lungs to the rest of the body. Anemia can result in reduced oxygen delivery to tissues and organs, leading to various symptoms and potential complications.

    Causes

    There are several types of anemia, each with different causes. Some of the common types include:

  • Iron-deficiency anemia: This is the most common type of anemia and occurs when the body lacks sufficient iron to produce an adequate amount of hemoglobin. Causes may include poor dietary iron intake, blood loss from menstruation or gastrointestinal bleeding, and certain medical conditions.
  • Vitamin-deficiency anemias: Deficiencies in vitamin B12, folate, and vitamin C can lead to different types of anemia. These vitamins play crucial roles in red blood cell production and maturation.
  • Hemolytic anemias: In these conditions, red blood cells are destroyed prematurely, either due to an inherited condition, infections, autoimmune disorders, or certain medications.
  • Aplastic anemia: This rare type of anemia occurs when the bone marrow fails to produce enough blood cells, including red blood cells. It can be acquired or inherited and may result from exposure to certain drugs, toxins, or infections.
  • Sickle cell anemia: This is a genetic disorder where the shape of red blood cells is abnormal, leading to a shorter lifespan and difficulty passing through blood vessels. It can cause episodes of pain and other complications.
  • Chronic diseases: Chronic diseases such as cancer, HIV/AIDS, rheumatoid arthritis, kidney disease, and other chronic inflammatory diseases can interfere with the production of red blood cells.
  • Aplastic anemia: This rare, life-threatening anemia occurs when your body doesn't produce enough red blood cells. Causes of aplastic anemia include infections, certain medicines, autoimmune diseases, and exposure to toxic chemicals.

    • Symptoms

    The symptoms of anemia can vary depending on its type and severity, but common signs include:

  • Fatigue and weakness
  • Shortness of breath
  • Pale or sallow skin
  • Cold hands and feet
  • Headache
  • Dizziness or light headedness
  • Irregular heartbeat
  • Chest pain

    • Treatment: Modern Medicine

     

  • Iron deficiency anemia: This is treated with iron supplements and changes to diet.
  • Vitamin deficiency anemias: Folic acid and vitamin B-12 supplements can correct these deficiencies.
  • Anemia of chronic disease: There's no specific treatment for this type of anemia. Doctors focus on treating the underlying disease.
  • Aplastic anemia: Treatment may include blood transfusions or a bone marrow transplant.
  • Anemias associated with bone marrow disease: Treatment might include medication, chemotherapy, or bone marrow transplantation.
  • Hemolytic anemias: Managing or avoiding the cause of the hemolysis is the main treatment strategy.
  • Pregnancy: Without iron supplementation, iron deficiency anemia occurs in many pregnant women because their iron stores need to serve their own increased blood volume as well as be a source of hemoglobin for the growing fetus.

    • . 

    Treatment: Traditional Medicine

  • Iron-Rich Foods: Consuming foods high in iron such as red meat, poultry, fish, lentils, beans, tofu, and dark green leafy vegetables can help increase iron levels in the body.
  •  Vitamin C: Consuming vitamin C-rich foods or taking a vitamin C supplement can enhance iron absorption. Good sources of vitamin C include citrus fruits, strawberries, bell peppers, and tomatoes.
  •  Blackstrap Molasses: Some people believe that blackstrap molasses, which is high in iron, can help combat anemia. It can be consumed in small amounts, but again, consulting a healthcare professional is essential.
  • Folate and Vitamin B12: Foods rich in folate (such as leafy green vegetables, citrus fruits, and fortified cereal) and vitamin B12 (such as eggs, dairy, fish, and fortified cereal) can be beneficial for certain types of anemia.
  • Herbal Remedies: Some herbs like nettle, dandelion, and yellow dock are thought to support iron levels. However, these should be used cautiously and under the guidance of a healthcare provider due to potential interactions with medications and side effects.

    •  

    Caution

  • Avoiding Certain Foods and Drinks: Some substances can hinder iron absorption, such as coffee, tea, and calcium-rich foods. It may be beneficial to avoid consuming these alongside iron-rich foods or supplements.
  • Untreated anemia can lead to severe fatigue, affecting daily activities.
  • During pregnancy, anemia (especially due to folate deficiency) can impact fetal development and lead to premature delivery.
  • Anemia strains the heart, causing rapid or irregular heartbeat. The heart compensates by pumping more blood, potentially leading to heart enlargement or failure.
  • Severe blood loss (e.g., road accidents) can be fatal

    • Prevention

     

    Some forms of anemia can't be prevented. However, you can avoid iron deficiency anemia and vitamin deficiency anemias by eating a diet that includes a variety of vitamins and minerals, including:

  • Iron: Meat, beans, and fortified cereals can help reduce the risk of iron deficiency.
  • Vitamin B-12: Meat, dairy products, and fortified cereals are good sources of B-12.
  • Folate (and its synthetic form, folic acid): This can be found in fruits and fruit juices, nuts, beans, peas, dairy products, meat, eggs, seafood, and grains.
