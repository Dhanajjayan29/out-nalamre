Peptic Ulcer

A peptic ulcer is a sore that develops on the lining of the stomach, small intestine, or esophagus. It occurs when the protective layer of mucus is eroded by stomach acid, leading to inflammation and damage to the tissues.

Causes and Risk Factors

Peptic ulcers are mainly caused by:

  • Infection with Helicobacter pylori bacteria.
  • Long-term use of nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) like ibuprofen and aspirin.
  • Excessive alcohol consumption or smoking.

Risk factors include:

  • Stress and an unhealthy diet.
  • Family history of peptic ulcers.

Symptoms

Common symptoms of peptic ulcers include:

  • A burning stomach pain or discomfort.
  • Bloating or belching.
  • Indigestion or nausea.
  • Weight loss and lack of appetite.

Treatment Options

The treatment for peptic ulcers may include:

Medications:
  • Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) to reduce stomach acid.
  • Antibiotics to treat Helicobacter pylori infection.
Surgical Options:
  • If the ulcer is severe or bleeding, surgery may be necessary to remove the ulcer or stop the bleeding.

Prevention Tips

The following can help reduce the risk of developing peptic ulcers:

  • Limit the use of NSAIDs and take them with food.
  • Quit smoking and reduce alcohol consumption.
  • Manage stress and maintain a healthy diet.
