GERD

GERD (Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease) is a digestive disorder where stomach acid or bile irritates the food pipe lining. It can cause symptoms such as heartburn, chest pain, and regurgitation.

Causes and Risk Factors

GERD occurs when the lower esophageal sphincter (LES) fails to close properly, allowing stomach contents to flow back into the esophagus. Common causes include:

  • Weakening of the LES.
  • Obesity, which can put pressure on the stomach.
  • Pregnancy can increase the likelihood of GERD.
  • Certain medications can relax the LES, leading to reflux.

Risk factors include:

  • Being overweight or obese.
  • Pregnancy.
  • Hiatal hernia.
  • Smoking and alcohol consumption.
  • Eating large meals or lying down right after a meal.

Symptoms

Common symptoms of GERD include:

  • Heartburn or a burning sensation in the chest.
  • Regurgitation of food or sour liquid.
  • Chest pain or discomfort.
  • Difficulty swallowing.

Treatment Options

The treatment for GERD may include:

Lifestyle Changes:
  • Avoiding foods that trigger acid reflux (such as spicy foods, citrus, and fried foods).
  • Eating smaller, more frequent meals rather than large meals.
  • Elevating the head of the bed during sleep to prevent reflux.
Medications:
  • Antacids to neutralize stomach acid.
  • Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) to reduce acid production.
  • H2 blockers to decrease stomach acid production.
Surgical Options:
  • If symptoms persist despite treatment, surgery may be considered to strengthen the LES or repair a hiatal hernia.

Prevention Tips

To reduce the risk of GERD, consider the following tips:

  • Avoid foods and drinks that trigger heartburn.
  • Maintain a healthy weight to reduce pressure on the stomach.
  • Quit smoking and limit alcohol consumption.
