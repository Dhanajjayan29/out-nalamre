    Astigmatism

    • Nalamaree Team
    • 25 April 2024

    Overview

    Astigmatism is a common refractive error of the eye that affects how light rays focus on the retina. Normally, the cornea and lens of the eye are smooth and curved equally in all directions, like the surface of a basketball. In astigmatism, the cornea or lens is curved more steeply in one direction than in the other, like the surface of a rugby ball. This irregular curvature causes light to focus unevenly, leading to blurred or distorted vision at all distances.

    Causes

    Astigmatism is not typically considered a disease; rather, it's a common refractive error of the eye. There isn't a single known cause of astigmatism, but it's believed to be primarily influenced by the shape of the cornea or lens. Some factors that may contribute to the development of astigmatism include:

  • Genetics: There's evidence to suggest that astigmatism can run in families, indicating a genetic component to its development.
  • Irregular shape of the cornea or lens: The cornea and lens should ideally be smoothly curved in all directions, but in astigmatism, they may be more curved in one direction than the other. This irregular shape can be present from birth or develop over time.
  • Eye injuries or surgeries: Trauma to the eye or certain eye surgeries can sometimes cause changes in the shape of the cornea, leading to astigmatism.
  • Keratoconus: This is a progressive condition in which the cornea becomes progressively thinner and cone-shaped, leading to astigmatism and other vision problems.
  • Certain conditions: Conditions such as cataracts or corneal scars can also lead to irregularities in the shape of the cornea or lens, resulting in astigmatism.

    • Symptoms

  • Blurred or distorted vision: Astigmatism can cause objects at all distances to appear blurry or distorted. This blurriness may not be consistent and can vary in different lighting conditions.
  • Eyestrain: Individuals with astigmatism may experience eyestrain, particularly after prolonged periods of reading, using digital devices, or performing tasks that require focusing.
  • Headaches: Eyestrain associated with astigmatism can lead to headaches, particularly around the forehead or temples.
  • Difficulty with night vision: Astigmatism can cause halos, glare, or difficulty seeing clearly in low-light conditions, such as when driving at night.
  • Squinting: Some people with astigmatism may unconsciously squint in an attempt to improve their vision by temporarily changing the shape of their eye's lens.
  • Eye discomfort: Astigmatism can sometimes cause eye discomfort or irritation, particularly if the condition is accompanied by dry eyes.

    • Treatment: Modern Medicine

  • Eyeglasses: Prescription eyeglasses with lenses that have a special cylindrical power can effectively correct astigmatism by compensating for the irregular curvature of the cornea or lens.
  • Contact lenses: Toric contact lenses are specifically designed to correct astigmatism. They have different powers in different meridians of the lens to counteract the uneven focusing of light caused by astigmatism.
  • Refractive surgery: Procedures like LASIK (laser-assisted in situ keratomileusis) and PRK (photorefractive keratectomy) can be used to reshape the cornea and correct astigmatism permanently. These surgeries use lasers to precisely reshape the cornea to improve its focusing ability.
  • Orthokeratology (Ortho-K): This is a non-surgical treatment where specially designed gas-permeable contact lenses are worn overnight to temporarily reshape the cornea. Ortho-K lenses are removed upon waking, and the reshaped cornea provides clear vision throughout the day.
  • Refractive lens exchange: In this surgical procedure, the eye's natural lens is replaced with an artificial intraocular lens (IOL) to correct refractive errors, including astigmatism. This procedure is often recommended for individuals with high degrees of astigmatism who may not be suitable candidates for LASIK or other laser surgeries.
  • Combination treatments: Some individuals may benefit from a combination of treatments, such as using toric contact lenses for everyday wear and undergoing refractive surgery for long-term correction.

    • Treatment: Traditional Medicine

  • Eye exercises: Some people believe that specific eye exercises can help strengthen eye muscles and improve focus, thereby reducing eyestrain associated with astigmatism. These exercises may include focusing on near and far objects, eye rolls, and palming (covering the eyes with the palms of the hands).
  • Proper lighting: Ensuring adequate lighting while reading, working on a computer, or performing other visually demanding tasks can reduce eyestrain and discomfort associated with astigmatism.
  • Eye relaxation techniques: Practices such as the 20-20-20 rule (taking a 20-second break to look at something 20 feet away every 20 minutes) can help reduce eye fatigue and strain.
  • Nutritional supplements: Consuming foods rich in nutrients beneficial for eye health, such as vitamin A, vitamin C, vitamin E, zinc, and omega-3 fatty acids, may support overall eye health. This includes foods like carrots, leafy greens, citrus fruits, nuts, seeds, and fatty fish.
  • Hydration: Staying adequately hydrated can help prevent dry eyes, which can exacerbate symptoms of astigmatism. Drinking plenty of water throughout the day is essential for maintaining optimal eye health.
  • Warm compresses: Applying a warm compress to the eyes can help alleviate dryness, irritation, and discomfort associated with astigmatism.
  • Good posture: Maintaining good posture while reading or using digital devices can help reduce strain on the eyes and alleviate discomfort.
  • Avoiding eye strain: Taking breaks from prolonged periods of close-up work, such as reading or using electronic devices, can help prevent eyestrain and discomfort.

    • Caution

  • Consultation with an eye care professional: Always consult with an optometrist or ophthalmologist before attempting any home remedies or alternative treatments for astigmatism. They can provide guidance tailored to your specific condition and overall eye health.
  • Risk of exacerbating symptoms: Some home remedies, such as eye exercises, may not be suitable for everyone and could potentially worsen symptoms or cause discomfort. It's crucial to use caution and discontinue any remedy that exacerbates your symptoms.
  • Safety concerns: Certain home remedies, such as using warm compresses, should be performed with caution to avoid injury or burns to the delicate tissues around the eyes. Follow proper instructions and use gentle, lukewarm compresses to avoid causing harm.
  • Limitations of home remedies: While traditional and home remedies may help alleviate symptoms of astigmatism, they are not a substitute for professional medical treatment. They may provide temporary relief but are unlikely to correct the underlying refractive error.

    • Prevention

  • Regular eye exams: Schedule regular comprehensive eye examinations with an optometrist or ophthalmologist, especially if you have a family history of astigmatism or other vision problems. Early detection and treatment of any refractive errors or eye conditions can help prevent complications and maintain good vision.
  • Eye protection: Wear protective eyewear, such as safety glasses or goggles, when participating in activities that pose a risk of eye injury, such as sports or certain occupations. Protecting the eyes from trauma can help reduce the risk of corneal irregularities that may lead to astigmatism.
  • Healthy lifestyle choices: Maintain a healthy lifestyle by eating a balanced diet rich in fruits, vegetables, and nutrients that support eye health, such as vitamin A, vitamin C, vitamin E, zinc, and omega-3 fatty acids. Stay hydrated, exercise regularly, and avoid smoking, which can contribute to various eye problems.
  • Proper ergonomics: Maintain proper posture and ergonomics when reading, using digital devices, or performing close-up work to reduce eye strain and fatigue. Follow the 20-20-20 rule—take a 20-second break to look at something 20 feet away every 20 minutes—to prevent eye strain during prolonged periods of near work.
  • Limit screen time: Minimize prolonged exposure to digital screens and take regular breaks to rest your eyes and prevent digital eye strain, which can exacerbate symptoms of astigmatism.
